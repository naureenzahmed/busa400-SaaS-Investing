Overview on Venture Capital

How Does a VC Firm Work?

What Does a Venture Capital Firm Do?

A venture capital (VC) firm is basically an investment manager. The main goal is to make a lot of money for the people and organizations that give it money. A VC firm is in the middle of a two-sided relationship. The Limited Partners (LPs) are the investors who give the money, and the General Partners (GPs) are the professionals who manage and invest that money in high-growth startups.

Pension funds, endowments, insurance companies, family offices, and high-net-worth individuals are all examples of large institutional investors that are LPs. There are a number of reasons why they put money into a VC fund. The main reason is money: historically, the best venture capital has made net returns of 20 to 30% per year, which is much higher than the long-term average for public equity markets. LPs invest in VC for more than just returns. They do it to get access to strategic co-investment opportunities, to diversify away from traditional asset classes like stocks and bonds, and to stay connected to new waves of innovation that could affect their business interests as a whole.

Cambridge Associates says that the US Venture Capital Index had a net return of 6.2% in 2024, bouncing back after two years of negative returns in 2022 and 2023. The picture is even more compelling over longer periods of time: the index made about 12% to 15% a year over the 25 years ending in 2023. The Cambridge Associates US VC Index made an average annual return of 32.4% from 1995 to 2020. The S&P 500, on the other hand, made an average annual return of 9.1% during the same time period. The problem is that these averages hide a lot of variation. The difference in net IRR between the top 25% and bottom 25% of VC funds is more than 30 percentage points, which is much bigger than in any other private equity strategy. In real life, picking the right fund is much more important than just putting money into the right asset class.

The high average returns on VC investments come from the very small chance of a big payoff. To get this average return without taking on a lot of risk, you need a very diverse portfolio. National Bureau of Economic Research

Why Do Venture Capitalists Put Money Into New Businesses?

The reason venture capitalists invest in startups is because they have an uneven return profile. Most startups fail, but the ones that do succeed can make a lot more money than is possible in public markets.

Facebook is a classic example of this dynamic. In 2005, when Accel Partners put money into Facebook at a value of about $100 million, the site was a small college social network with no proven business model. When Facebook went public in 2012, it was worth about $100 billion. Accel's investment had grown by about 1,000 times its original value. On the other hand, an investor who bought Facebook shares on the public market at the IPO price would have made about three times their money over the next ten years. This is a good return, but it's not even close to the venture-level upside.

This is the main reason why venture capital is an asset class. VCs are okay with illiquidity, uncertainty, and high failure rates because they want to be able to invest in the next big company as soon as possible, when prices are lowest and ownership stakes are highest.

Most startups (90%) fail in the end. But one big investment can pay off many times over for a whole fund. The VC model is based on this power-law dynamic, which is why portfolio construction and diversification are so important.
Harvard Business School / Failory Research, 2024

Why Put Money Into More Than One Startup?

Startups lose money on cash flow; they need outside funding all the time to stay alive. A single bad outcome, like a technical failure, a market rejection, or a competitive disruption, can completely destroy an investment. Because of this, VCs make sure their portfolios are diverse. Each fund usually has between 10 and 30 companies, which helps spread risk across many positions.

This diversification is necessary because of the statistical reality of venture returns. Data on 21,640 venture financings from 2004 to 2013 shows that 64.8% of the investments returned between zero and one times the money put in, which means they were losses. Another 25.3% gave back between one and five times the original investment. Only 5.9% came back 5 to 10 times, and only 0.4% came back more than 50 times. At the median, a $100 million portfolio that invested in 10 companies would make about $206 million, or a 10% internal rate of return. Also, if you take out the investments that made more than five times the money you put in, that same portfolio has a negative return. This is the most important thing to know about venture investing: a small number of outliers support the whole fund.

According to research from Harvard Business School, about 75% of startups that get money from investors never give it back. Investors lose all of their money in 30 to 40% of cases. This is how the system works: the few breakout companies make up for the many that never reached their full potential.

How Is a VC Fund Structured?

The way a venture capital fund works follows a clear cycle that usually lasts ten years, but it can be extended by one year if needed. To understand how VCs make decisions, why they act the way they do, and what makes them interested in a particular startup, you need to know this cycle.

Stage 1
Raising Money

The GP starts the process by getting money from LPs. Banks, corporations, endowments, sovereign wealth funds, and wealthy people are all sources of capital. Fundraising is a constant process. When one fund's investment period ends, the GP starts to raise the next one. This puts constant pressure on LPs to show that they are doing well and keep good relationships with LPs.

Stage 2
Investment and Evaluation

The GP starts looking at startup opportunities and putting money to work after the fund has been raised. The commitment period is the first five years of the fund's life. This is usually the only time when new investments are made. The due diligence section below goes into great detail about how to evaluate a startup.

Stage 3
Keeping an Eye on Things

After they make an investment, the VC is in charge of running the portfolio company and making sure it grows profitably. This includes being on the board, giving strategic advice, making introductions to potential customers and partners, and helping to hire senior staff. Putting money into a startup is just the first step; VCs make more money by helping the founder grow their business.

Stage 4
Exit

An exit event creates liquidity. This event usually happens in one of three ways: an initial public offering (IPO), a merger or acquisition (M&A), or, in the worst case, a failure and wind-down. The Dow Jones VentureSource database tracks data that shows that about 12% of early-stage investors' portfolio companies go public (IPO) and about 19% go through a merger or acquisition (M&A). About 38% of companies that reach exit fail. Late-stage investors, who usually put money into older companies, see higher rates of mergers and acquisitions (50%) and initial public offerings (60%). There were a lot fewer exits in North America and Europe between 2014 and 2016, because companies stayed private longer, and this trend has continued into the current decade.

Stage 5
Distribution

After exits happen, capital and investment proceeds are sent back to both LPs and GPs according to a waterfall structure that was agreed upon ahead of time. This distribution means that the fund's life cycle is over.

How Long Does a Typical Venture Fund Last?

Most Limited Partnership Agreements (LPAs) let a standard venture fund last for ten years, but they can be extended for up to three one-year periods, bringing the total to thirteen years. The GP makes new investments during the first five years, which is called the commitment or investment period. After the fifth year, the fund goes into its harvesting phase. This means that no new companies are added to the portfolio, but the GP keeps investing in existing portfolio companies and works hard to get them to leave.

A new fund is usually raised between the third and fifth years of the current fund, before the first fund's investment period ends. Because of this overlap, a GP often has to manage an existing portfolio while raising and deploying a new fund at the same time. This takes a lot of bandwidth and organizational structure.

The fund's life can be divided into three distinct phases:

  • Formation (Year 0): Getting money, setting up the legal structure, and setting up the taxes. The GP sets up the fund entity, works out the terms with the LP, and does all the initial planning.
  • Investment (Years 0 to 5): Finding deals, doing due diligence, putting money into new investments, and making early follow-on investments. The investment team is busiest during this phase of operations.
  • Harvesting (Years 5 to 10+): Getting the portfolio ready for exit, helping portfolio companies grow, making follow-on investments in the best performers, and finally giving LPs their money back.

How Do VCs Make Money?

There are two ways that GPs get paid: a management fee and carried interest, which is often called "carry."

The management fee is usually 2% of the committed capital per year. This fee covers the fund's operating costs, such as salaries, rent, travel, and legal fees. This makes $2 million a year, or about $20 million over the ten-year life of a $100 million fund. This fee is not a profit; it's part of the operating budget.

Carried interest is how GPs make real money. Carry is usually 20% of the fund's profits over a hurdle rate, which is usually around 8%. This is also known as a preferred return. The mechanics work like this, using a simple example of a top-decile fund:

  • The LPs give the fund $100 million.
  • After paying $20 million in fees and costs, $80 million is put into portfolio companies.
  • Of those investments, $20 million is written off as losses, so there is still $60 million in active holdings.
  • The remaining portfolio grows to a value of $240 million, which is three times the amount of money that was invested.
  • After paying back the $80 million in invested capital, the $20 million in fees, and any preferred return obligations, there is still $120 million left.
  • Out of that $120 million, LPs get 80% ($96 million) and the GP gets 20% ($24 million) as carried interest.

This structure makes sure that GPs and LPs have the same goals: GPs only get paid well when their investments make more money than the hurdle rate. A fund that only returns capital doesn't make the GP any money other than the management fee.

How to Source a Deal

What Does Deal Sourcing Mean for a VC Firm?

When it comes to venture capital, not all companies are the same. Deal flow, or the number, quality, and timing of investment opportunities that a firm can get, is a big part of what makes a top-decile fund different from a median fund. Deal sourcing is the first step in the whole venture capital process. Without it, even the most thorough due diligence process has nothing to work with. That's why venture capital (VC) firms try to reach as many people as possible to find out if they are making the best investments in the market.

The best people in the industry are aware of this dynamic. Top venture capital firms don't just sit around and wait for founders to come to them. They create proactive, multi-channel sourcing engines that look for the best startups as soon as possible, sometimes even before those startups have any public profile.

How Does the Competition Look for Finding Deals?

Data from the National Venture Capital Association and PitchBook show that US VC managers made more than 14,600 deals and spent more than $213 billion in 2024 alone. But even with this many deals, the basic truth about venture investing hasn't changed: most of the returns come from a very small number of companies.

Because returns are so concentrated, getting the right deals is very important. The people who started those companies with a lot of potential also know how much power they have. The best founders often get more than one term sheet at once, and the price is not the only thing that affects their decision about which company to work with. A founder's choice of investor depends on the brand, network, operational support, and speed of execution. This is why deal sourcing is a two-way competition: VCs not only want to find the best startups, but they also want to convince those startups to take their money instead of other options.

The ecosystem has grown too big, spread out too much, and moved too quickly for one person or a small team to cover it all through personal relationships alone. Because of this, top companies have spent a lot of money on building systematic, scalable sourcing infrastructure that works with traditional relationship-driven approaches and adds new tools, programs, and data capabilities.

Scout Programs

A VC firm works with a distributed network of operators, founders, academics, and investors in innovation hotspots around the world as part of a scout program. These scouts are paid, usually with a small share of the carry in deals they find, to find and recommend high-potential founders to the firm before those founders start a formal fundraising process.

The scout program at Sequoia Capital is one of the most famous in the business. It has been said that it has brought in deals that would never have made it to the firm's funnel through normal channels. The program works because scouts have real, trustworthy connections in their own communities that other VC firms can't make from far away. Also, scouts bring different points of view that can help find opportunities in communities that aren't well-represented and with founders who don't fit the mold. Scout programs also add an extra layer of evaluation because scouts do an informal pre-screening before sending companies to you. This makes the leads you get at the top of the funnel better.

Funds for Students

Student funds are a complementary strategy that focuses on the university campus, which is the first step in starting a business. These vehicles are driven by trained college students and use pre-seed capital to help promising student entrepreneurs who are still in school start businesses. In North America, Dorm Room Fund and Front Row Ventures are two well-known examples of this model. Student fund programs also help train the next generation of venture capitalists by providing a steady stream of trained analysts and associates who know how the firm invests. They do due diligence on very early-stage opportunities at a low cost because student fund teams do the first evaluation work before a deal gets to the sponsoring firm.

Venture Studios

Venture studios, also known as startup studios or company builders, build, launch, and run several startups at the same time from within the company itself. The studio has everything you need to run multiple new businesses at once, including product teams, designers, legal support, marketing tools, and access to a network of experts.

The model has a number of strong benefits for VC firms that run or work with venture studios. The startup process is less risky because experienced operators are involved from the beginning and use what they learned from building other companies. When resources are shared, it lowers the burn rates across the portfolio by creating economies of scale. Before making any formal investment decision, it has a very clear view of the team's skills, the product's progress, and the business model's viability. Companies that are built in a studio tend to have a higher survival rate than companies that are started on their own, because the model gives them structural support.

Feeder Funds

Feeder funds let established VC firms reach more people by investing in new fund managers who are putting money into markets, geographies, or verticals by backing smaller, more specialized funds. This gives them access to their deal flow, learnings, and portfolio companies.

The Discovery Fund from Inovia Capital is a well-known example of this model in action. It focuses on investing in new managers to make the Canadian tech funding ecosystem stronger. The fund sets up a win-win situation: new managers get capital and institutional credibility, and Inovia gets to see a wider range of companies and markets than it could through direct investment alone. The feeder fund model also helps reduce risk by giving the investor exposure to a variety of managers and strategies instead of putting all their eggs in one basket.

Service Offerings

Some of the biggest VC firms in the world have built whole platforms of services to draw in founders and keep in touch with the startup ecosystem. Andreessen Horowitz (a16z) helps its portfolio companies run their businesses by giving them access to curated networks and events, operational support, advisory services through a library of company-building content, platform services, and technical expertise. These services are a way for the company to find deals because they give founders a reason to work with them even before they need money.

Service platforms get founders interested in the company by showing that it can be more than just a source of money. A company that is known for really helping founders will attract better founders over time, creating a cycle of deal quality improvement that keeps going.

EIRs and Referral Networks

Entrepreneurs in Residence (EIRs) are experienced business owners or former founders who work for a company for a set amount of time. During that time, they may give feedback on how existing portfolio companies are doing, look into new investment opportunities, or work on starting a new company that the firm will invest in. The best thing about the EIR model for finding deals is that when an EIR starts a new business, the founding team is already vetted. The firm already knows the person's skills, work ethic, and judgment in a way that cold inbound deal flow can't.

Zapflow's research shows that referral networks always offer the best investment opportunities for VC firms. This is because the person who refers the opportunity has already done some informal screening and is putting their own reputation on the line by making the introduction. Leads from referral networks are usually pre-vetted, come with useful information, and turn into investments at much higher rates than cold inbounds.

Conferences and Events for Networking

Conferences bring together investors, founders, service providers, and ecosystem players in settings that encourage casual conversation and exploration. Being at important events on a regular basis builds the company's reputation and brand in the ecosystem, showing that they are serious about a certain area or sector. It makes connections that often pay off months or years later, when the founder decides to raise money. One of the less well-known parts of a strong sourcing strategy is how consistent event presence over time can have a compounding effect. "In real life" is the only thing that sets them apart, especially now that AI and automation are common. It also touches on the basics of human nature, like the need for support and connection. When founders are having a hard time and need help, they can call the partner who is there to help them.

Steps in the Investment Process

Investing in startups is a structured process that starts with finding an opportunity and ends with becoming a trusted partner in the founder's journey. Every step helps you feel more sure of yourself and gives you an edge over your competitors.

Step 1
Finding the Deal

The best venture capitalists create multi-channel sourcing engines that include referral networks, scout programs, student funds, conferences, and data-driven inbound platforms. The goal is to spot the best opportunities early, before competition drives up prices and makes it harder to own.

Step 2
Thesis Fit

Before spending any time with a company, a VC must ask themselves one question: does this company fit within our fund's investment mandate? Thesis alignment includes stage, sector, geography, and check size. If a deal is objectively strong but not in the fund's area of expertise, it should be passed on. This is because investments that are not aligned with the fund's goals can weaken its expertise and stretch its resources because they can't invest because of their LPAC.

Step 3
Due Diligence

Due diligence is focused, not all-encompassing. The most important factors are the team (95% of VCs say this is the most important), the market (it should be big, growing, and not well served), the competition (it should be easy to tell the difference), the product (it should show real value creation), the business model (it should have sound unit economics and a scalable go-to-market), and the risks (what needs to go right and what could go wrong). The process is based on chance, not certainty. The goal is to come up with a strong investment thesis, not to get rid of all doubt. It is also to see if the team can handle the uncertainties and do what they need to do.

Step 4
The Investment Committee

There are two ways to approve deals: the conviction model, where one partner can champion and close an investment, and the consensus model, where most or all partners must agree. In either case, the partner who is sponsoring the project must be able to explain a clear investment thesis and a strong case for getting a return. The best IC presentations show that the hardest questions have already been asked and that there are no objections.

Step 5
Getting the Founder

The best founders pick their investors as carefully as investors pick their founders. A VC wins allocation by showing that they can offer more than just money, like making useful introductions to people in their network, sharing their knowledge of the industry, giving operational advice, and having a history of helping founders through tough times. Founders want more than just money; they want a partner.

Step 6
Winning Allocation

In competitive rounds, you get allocation based on how fast you are, how well you get along with others, and how much value you think you add. Getting to the term sheet first, offering better terms than other bidders, or giving a strategic asset that no one else has, like introducing a key customer or putting a board member with direct category expertise on the board, can make or break a fund's ability to get its target ownership.

Step 7
Making Your Portfolio Worth More

Active board participation, help with hiring the right people, introductions to customers and investors, and honest advice on how to run the business all add to the value of an investment. The VC's return depends on how well the founder does, so they have a duty to help after the investment both as a fiduciary and as a matter of reputation.

These seven steps don't make a linear list; instead, they show how rigor, judgment, and trust build on each other.

How Startups Can Extract Value from VC Firms

There is a well-known hole in the middle of venture capital. The Forward More Than Money report from 2021 says that 92% of VC firms say they are value-add investors. Only 61% of founders agree, and 65% say their investors tried but failed. It's clear what this means: giving value beyond money isn't automatic. It needs to be planned, structured, and really focused on the founder.

1. Introductions to Customers and Networks

Access to networks is the type of VC support that people value the most. A study found that 33% of founders say that an investor's connections are a key factor in the success of a business (Forward More Than Money, 2021). This includes introductions to potential customers, strategic partners, and ways to get the word out about your business. A VC who starts a meaningful business relationship with a startup can help it get to market months faster. Notably, 63% of US-based founders say that customer introductions are one of the top three things they look for when choosing a VC.

2. Help with Fundraising

Help getting more money in the future is the second most valuable thing. Founders see VCs as natural ways to get to the next round. In a competitive fundraising environment, a VC's warm introductions to co-investors, its signal of continued confidence through pro-rata participation, and its coaching on investor narrative and data room preparation can make all the difference. Jolyon Martin from PetMedix has said that this is the best thing an investor can do to add value.

3. Finding and Hiring Talented People

For most early-stage companies, hiring is the hardest part of running their business. Forbes (2025) reports on research that shows that portfolio companies that get active talent support from their VCs cut the time it takes to hire by 40% and raise the acceptance rates of executives by 60%. VCs can use their networks to find candidates, cut down on the need for expensive recruiters, and help founders tell equity stories that draw in top talent.

4. Advice on Strategy and Operations

VC investors can spot patterns across many portfolios that founders, who only work for one company, can't. This includes guides on how to set prices, how to sell, how to prioritize product development, and how to deal with crises. Research from ScienceDirect shows that VC-backed companies with active investor monitoring see much higher productivity growth in the years after their first round of investment. This is because of coaching and value-adding services, not just the money itself.

5. Help with the Board and Governance

When board members are involved, investors can hold people accountable in a structured way. Founders get a governance structure that grows with the company from VCs who come prepared, question assumptions in a helpful way, and help set realistic operating goals. The ScienceDirect literature on VC monitoring further establishes that closer VC-CEO interaction is most valuable at the earliest stages, when uncertainty is highest and the need for external perspective is greatest.

6. Community of Portfolio and Peer Learning

A good VC firm adds value to the whole portfolio, not just the individual companies. More and more people are realizing that connecting founders who are facing similar growth challenges, helping portfolio companies share knowledge, and putting on events that encourage peer learning are all important ways to stand out.

Companies that close the gap between what they promise and what founders actually get are the ones that plan ahead for post-investment support instead of leaving it up to chance.

Finding the Right Investment

How to Match a Firm's Investment Thesis and Portfolio to Potential Deals

A venture capital firm's investment thesis is basically the backbone of how it makes decisions. It deeply describes out what the firm believes about where the world is heading, which markets are worth betting on, and what kinds of companies the partners are genuinely well-positioned to help. Matching that thesis to incoming deals works as both a filtering tool and a reflection of what the firm is actually good at and focuses on. Without this kind of alignment, firms end up diluting their value-add and leaving LPs confused about what they actually believe in.

Fred Wilson of Union Square Ventures has written a lot about the difference between thematic and thesis-driven investing. Thematic investing picks up on broad macro trends like mobility, decentralization, or AI, and spreads bets across companies that ride those waves. Thesis-driven investing is more specific. It makes a concrete, testable claim about how a market will evolve, then looks for companies that fit that picture. USV's well-known thesis around large networks of engaged users, differentiated through user experience and defensible through network effects, guided investments in Twitter, Etsy, and Coinbase. Every deal they looked at was held against that standard first. Julius Bachmann points out in Hackernoon that this kind of specificity is what lets early-stage firms move fast and with real conviction. When you know what you are looking for, you recognize it quickly, because it becomes pattern matching within the winner of that industry and stage.

Matching a deal to a thesis is not just running through a checklist. Investors at firms like Andreessen Horowitz and Sequoia actively map their existing portfolio before sourcing new deals. To expose themselves to as much diversification as possible, they ask things like: where are we underexposed? What stage, geography, or sector are we missing? Little Engine Ventures calls this a portfolio gap analysis, which involves reviewing current positions to figure out where a new investment would add optionality or balance out existing concentration. If a fund is already heavily weighted toward B2B SaaS, piling into another deal in the same space might not just be redundant. It could create conflicts of interest or reduce the portfolio's ability to generate outsized outcomes.

The thesis also sends a signal to founders. Sam Altman has noted that the best founders in any category tend to seek out investors who genuinely understand their market. A firm without a clear thesis often ends up seeing the deals that nobody else wanted. Thesis clarity works as both an evaluation filter and a sourcing engine. It pulls in the exact founders the firm is best set up to support.

Beyond thesis fit, portfolio matching means thinking carefully about pro-rata rights, signaling risk, and stage concentration. If a firm has already written big Series A checks across a sector, leading another seed deal in that same space might read to the market as a sign the Series A position is struggling. According to Contrary Research, portfolio construction discipline requires intentional sequencing. It is about knowing not just which deals to do, but when and in what proportion. Matching thesis to deals is really an act of active portfolio management, not just picking individual companies.

How VC Firms Pick Investments and the Factors of Consideration

VC investment involve fast pattern-matching against past experience, genuine conviction about teams, and a real structural read on market opportunity. Underneath what looks like pure gut feel, most high-performing firms are actually applying pretty consistent frameworks. Understanding how those frameworks work is useful for anyone who operates in or around the venture world.

Market Size

Market size is usually the first thing investors look at. A16z's foundational piece on startup metrics (2015) emphasized that investors need to assess total addressable market with some healthy skepticism. The market has to be big enough to support a venture-scale return. A $1B market can produce a solid business, but it rarely produces a fund-returning company. Mike Vernal of Sequoia has written about the market curve, pointing out that the best companies often create their own markets rather than just capturing existing ones. That means evaluating not just where the TAM is today, but where it plausibly goes as the company itself grows.

Team Evaluation

Team evaluation is the biggest factor at early stages. First Round Capital's diligence process stresses that product intuition, domain expertise, and founder-market fit are often more predictive than the business model itself. Investors want founders who have spent real time close to the problem, not just intellectually but in practice. The core question is whether they have a genuinely differentiated point of view and the grit to survive when early assumptions turn out to be wrong. The team needs to be able to figure it out when all goes wrong and all the odds are stacked against them.

Product and Technology Differentiation

Product and technology differentiation comes right after. GoingVC's due diligence framework recommends checking whether the core tech is proprietary, whether the product is solving a real pain or just a nice-to-have, and whether the unit economics hold up at scale.

Competitive Dynamics and Timing

Competitive dynamics and timing also matter a lot. Arteen Arabshahi's marketplace calculator framework shows that for marketplace businesses, whether the unit economics ever close depends a lot on the structural balance between supply and demand, and which side the company actually controls. A company entering a market a year too early burns cash building supply-side infrastructure nobody is ready to use. A year too late and they cannot displace an incumbent with strong network effects. The EC Early Stage diligence framework explicitly scores timing as a variable for this reason.

Valuation

Even a great company is a bad investment if the price is wrong. A16z's 16 startup metrics framework includes burn multiple, revenue growth rate, and net dollar retention as core diligence inputs. A company growing at 3x per year but burning 10x revenue to get there is not building durable value. It is basically borrowing growth against future dilution. Price discipline is what keeps disciplined venture from sliding into speculative capital deployment.

Considerations for Portfolio Construction Fund Over Fund

Portfolio construction in venture capital is genuinely complex, and it gets more complex across fund vintages. Each fund has to balance power-law return dynamics, stage concentration, sector diversification, reserve management, and LP expectations while staying consistent with the firm's investment thesis. Done poorly, a firm can produce strong individual deals but mediocre fund-level returns. Done well, portfolio construction sets up the conditions for at least one company to return the entire fund.

The foundational insight here comes from power law math. Jerry Neumann's analysis at Reaction Wheel shows clearly that VC returns are not normally distributed. The top one or two investments in a portfolio typically account for a wildly disproportionate share of total returns. This means concentration and portfolio size pull against each other. A portfolio of 40 seed investments has better statistical coverage than one of 10, but if you write smaller checks to get that breadth, you may not own enough to matter when a winner emerges. Fred Wilson has noted that USV typically targets 20 to 25 companies per fund, large enough to expect a few breakouts, small enough to maintain meaningful ownership and stay engaged at the board level.

Reserve ratios are probably the most consequential operational decision in portfolio construction. The ICONIQ SaaS Glossary notes that institutional funds typically reserve 50 to 60 percent of fund capital for follow-on investments. The companies most deserving of follow-on capital are the ones already in your portfolio performing well. Not reserving enough forces you to either dilute at later rounds or pass on pro-rata rights, both of which erode your position in the companies that matter most.

Fund-over-fund construction also means learning from vintage-level mistakes. If Fund I was overweighted in consumer, and consumer returned poorly, Fund II might shift toward enterprise SaaS as a reactive bet, but also as a considered response to where the partners actually have an informational edge. Mostly Metrics makes the point that SaaS businesses specifically benefit from investors who can benchmark metrics against cohorts of similar-stage companies. Furthermore, firms that have deployed two or more funds into SaaS accumulate that benchmarking advantage.

Loss ratios need to be anticipated and built into the construction logic. Wilson from AVC writes that in early-stage VC, roughly 40 to 50 percent of investments will result in a loss of capital, 20 to 30 percent will return capital or a small multiple, and 20 to 30 percent will generate meaningful returns. The power law math means this distribution is not a failure. It is the expected outcome of a diversified early-stage portfolio. A firm that engineers its portfolio specifically to minimize loss ratios is probably over-indexing on safety and underweighting the big bets that return funds.

Across funds, the path from Fund I to Fund III typically involves increasing specialization, tighter ownership targets, and more disciplined reserve policies. Battery Ventures' State of the OpenCloud highlights that cloud software portfolios built with discipline around ARR growth benchmarks and net retention thresholds tend to produce more consistent fund-level returns than generalist approaches. Each fund vintage is a proof point, not just to LPs, but to the partners themselves, about what their actual edge is.

How Portfolio Adjustments Are Made in Changing Market Conditions

Market dislocations like rising interest rates, contracting public multiples, tightening LP capital, or sector-specific corrections force GPs to rethink their portfolio strategy in real time. How a firm manages its portfolio during downturns is honestly just as consequential as how it picks investments during good times. The calls around down rounds, reserve deployment, triage, and follow-on pacing can determine whether a fund's best companies actually survive long enough to become defining outcomes.

When a company raises at a valuation below its prior round, existing investors face dilution, anti-dilution provisions kick in, and the signal to the market is not great. The Equals SaaS Metrics Guide notes that in a down round environment, the key metric to watch is burn multiple, which tracks how many dollars of capital are burned to generate each new dollar of net new ARR. A company with a burn multiple above 2x in a down market is unlikely to attract outside capital without meaningfully restructuring its operations. Investors have to make a call: does this company have a real path to profitability with a bridge, or is putting more money in just delaying the inevitable?

Reserve deployment becomes a lot more strategic during market corrections. Firms that over-deployed reserves into follow-ons during the 2021 bull market found themselves without dry powder when portfolio companies needed bridge financing in 2022 and 2023. The lesson from that period, covered well by Axios Pro Rata, is that reserve pacing needs to be countercyclical. You want to be holding reserves for when companies need capital most, which is exactly when external capital is hardest to raise. A common rule of thumb is to reserve enough for at least two additional rounds for each core portfolio position.

Cutting out, meaning declining to participate in a follow-on, is one of the harder decisions a VC faces. Not following on sends a signal to co-investors and to the market. However, continuing to invest in underperforming companies just to avoid sending that signal is what gets called zombie financing, and it eats fund returns while consuming GP time. GoingVC's due diligence framework suggests developing explicit decision criteria before you even make an investment. Under what conditions will you not follow on? Forcing that conversation during initial underwriting rather than in the middle of a tense board meeting produces cleaner decisions later.

When a sector re-prices, the same company that looked expensive at Series B can suddenly look quite reasonable. SaaS multiples compressing from 15x ARR to 6x ARR is a good example. The Pmarchive writing from Marc Andreessen makes the point that market timing is less predictable than market quality, and that the best time to invest in a great company is often when others are pulling back. Firms with dry powder during contractions should generally be leaning in.

On the operational side, portfolio adjustments during downturns require a triage mindset. Wilson suggests bucketing portfolio companies into three groups: those that will survive regardless of support, those that need help and capital to have a real shot, and those that are unlikely to return capital no matter what the firm does. GP time is the scarcest resource in the building. Allocating it heavily toward the middle bucket, the companies that are actually salvageable, while accepting the write-offs in the third group, is the most value-maximizing approach. Downturns are painful, but they do clarify which companies were built on real foundations and which were just products of cheap capital.

Important Considerations for the SaaS Market

How Did Innovation Affect the SaaS Market?

The thing that changed everything was AI. High Alpha's 2025 SaaS Benchmarks Report, which draws on data from over 800 companies, found that of respondents whose companies were founded in 2016, none reported AI as core to their product. Companies founded in 2025 were at 100 percent. Innovation in SaaS right now is basically a single question: how deeply is AI actually embedded in what the product does?

The firms tracking this most closely Bessemer Venture Partners, Battery Ventures, and High Alpha are consistent on one point. Bolting AI onto an existing product is not the same as building natively around it. Bessemer's State of the Cloud 2024 noted that AI-native portfolio companies are reaching $1 billion in revenue roughly 50 percent faster than their legacy cloud counterparts. OpenAI reportedly crossed a $3.4 billion run rate within months of reaching $2 billion. That pace of commercial scaling has no precedent in SaaS history.

Vertical SaaS is the other innovation story worth paying attention to. The old model was horizontal build a broad platform and sell it to everyone. The newer pattern is going deep in a single industry, healthcare, legal, construction, dental, and owning that workflow entirely. The moat is not network effects, it is workflow lock-in and proprietary data. Competitors cannot replicate three years of industry-specific data accumulation just by shipping a better UI. For investors evaluating innovation in a SaaS market, the question is not just whether the product uses AI, but whether the AI is trained on data that nobody else has access to creating massive defensibility.

Usage-based pricing is also a structural innovation, not just a monetization choice. Metronome's 2025 State of Usage-Based Pricing report found 85 percent of SaaS companies had adopted or were testing it. And the performance data supports the shift. Usage-based models deliver 10 percent higher NRR, 22 percent lower churn, and two times faster growth compared to flat seat-based pricing. The reason is alignment. When a customer pays based on what they actually consume, the incentive to engage deeply is real. The product has to keep delivering value or revenue walks out with the customer. This constantly growing pressure makes better products.

How Is Consumer Traction Changing SaaS?

Consumer traction in SaaS is not the same as user acquisition. A lot of teams confuse the two and it costs them. Getting people to sign up is a marketing problem. Getting them to stick around and pay more over time is a product problem. The metrics that actually tell you whether you have traction are retention, expansion, and NPS not logo count or MRR on the day a customer signs.

The 2025 High Alpha SaaS Benchmarks Report put it clearly. Companies that pair high NRR with low CAC nearly double their growth rates and Rule of 40 scores compared to peers with weaker retention or longer payback periods. Even modest increases in NRR can offset higher CAC. The inverse rarely holds. You cannot CAC your way out of a churn problem. When investors talk about consumer traction in SaaS they are really asking whether the product creates enough value that customers expand their usage over time, and whether the unit economics of that expansion are better than the economics of new acquisition. If they are, you have a flywheel. If they are not, you have a leaky bucket with a good sales team.

Product-led growth changed how traction looks in the early stages. OpenView's research has consistently found that PLG companies grow faster and with lower CAC than sales-led equivalents at the same ARR range. The mechanism is user-level adoption that grows into account-level revenue. A developer or analyst starts using a tool on a free tier, finds value, brings it into their team, and the account expands without a single SDR call. That pattern user-initiated bottoms-up adoption moving toward enterprise contracts is what investors mean when they ask about organic traction. It is also the hardest thing to fake because it shows up in cohort data, not just headline ARR.

SMB versus enterprise traction tells you different things about the product. SMBs adopt fast and churn fast. High SMB NPS with high churn usually means the product is genuinely useful but not sticky enough to survive budget cuts. Enterprise traction is slower to build, but when it holds, it holds for years. The ChartMogul 2024 SaaS Retention Report found that achieving NRR of 100 percent or above is increasingly difficult across all ARR ranges, even at the top quartile of companies with $15M to $30M in ARR. The benchmark has moved. What passed as strong retention in 2021 is now table stakes at best, and in many categories it is not even that.

What Are the Changes in the SaaS Market?

The market changed sharply after 2021 and it has not fully snapped back to what it was. The 2020 to 2021 period was an anomaly. Zero interest rates, forced digital adoption from the pandemic, and a bull market in public SaaS valuations pushed growth rates and fundraising conditions to levels that were not structurally sustainable. Then, interest rates went up, multiples were compressed, and companies that had built on growth-at-all-costs assumptions found themselves exposed. The SaaS metrics benchmark data from KeyBanc, covering 104 companies with median $26M ARR, showed median ARR growth settling at 19 to 21 percent in 2024. In 2021 those same companies would have been at 40 to 60 percent and considered underperformers.

The composition of what is getting funded has changed too. High Alpha's 2025 Benchmarks Report noted that while deal value has returned to 2021 highs, deal count is still at 2022 and 2023 levels. The capital is concentrating in very few players driving their valuations high. AI accounts for a disproportionate share of total deal value with average deal sizes exceeding $40 million. For SaaS companies that are not clearly AI-first or AI-native, the fundraising environment is harder than headline numbers suggest. Capital is available it is just going to a narrower set of companies, creating a significant gap in fundraising for others.

Pricing is also shifting in ways that change market dynamics. SaaS Capital's 2025 data found that average SaaS price increases ran at 8.7 percent annually, ranging from 10 to 25 percent across categories roughly five times the G7 market inflation rate. Paddle's research found 42 to 73 percent of SaaS companies raised or planned to raise prices in 2024 with a median increase of around 20 percent. The Vena Solutions SaaS statistics report found that on average five people oversee a SaaS purchase, with the finance team involved 44 percent of the time. That is not the frictionless bottom-up adoption story of 2019. Buyers are more careful and more skeptical, and that changes how traction is built and measured.

Bain's early 2026 analysis of the public SaaS selloff made the competitive shift structural. AI tools replicating core functionality at lower cost are putting pressure on gross retention across categories where switching costs have historically been low. If gross retention for incumbent SaaS products drops below the 90 percent level that used to be standard, the LTV math deteriorates rapidly. The market is splitting between products with deep workflow integration and proprietary data moats which are holding up and generic SaaS delivery models, which are exposed.

SaaS Evaluation Metrics

What Are the Main Components and Metrics Looked at in Venture-Backed SaaS Companies?

From the moment a SaaS company acquires its first customer, it is burning more cash than it takes in. The initial sale rarely covers the cost of acquiring that customer. Instead, the company recoups that upfront investment over time through recurring subscription fees. This means earnings look terrible early on even when the business is actually healthy.

CAC Customer Acquisition Cost

The most foundational metric in this picture is Customer Acquisition Cost, or CAC. In a SaaS context, CAC is how much it costs in total sales and marketing spend to bring in one new subscriber. There are two versions worth tracking. One looks only at program costs like ad spend and events. The other is fully-loaded and includes the salaries and overhead of the entire sales and marketing team. The fully-loaded CAC is the more conservative and honest number, and it tends to look a lot worse in the early days of a SaaS business when the team is expensive, but the customer base is still small.

LTV Lifetime Value

On the other side of the equation is Lifetime Value, or LTV. In a SaaS business, LTV estimates how much gross profit a customer will generate over the entire duration of their subscription. LTV should be based on gross margin, because the actual cost to serve a SaaS customer including hosting, support, and infrastructure meaningfully reduces what you are actually keeping from each subscription dollar. The rule of thumb that most investors use is that LTV should be at least three times CAC for the business to be structurally viable. If you are spending more acquiring customers than you will ever make back from them on a gross margin basis, the subscription model is not working regardless of how fast revenue is growing.

ARR Annual Recurring Revenue

Annual Recurring Revenue is the core revenue metric for SaaS. It converts the value of all active subscriptions into an annualized run rate, giving a cleaner picture of the revenue base than booking or billings numbers. The more useful view breaks ARR into its moving parts: Gross New ARR (total) and Expansion ARR (from existing customers who upgraded or added seats), Contraction ARR (downgrades), and Churned ARR (cancellations). Looking at each component separately matters because a company can report strong Net ARR while quietly losing its best customers, masked by aggressive new logo acquisition. That is a very different and much more dangerous business than one where the ARR growth is coming from a healthy mix of new customers and expansion.

Retention GDR and NDR

Retention is where the SaaS model either proves itself or falls apart. There are two main retention metrics investors track: GDR and NDR. Gross Dollar Retention measures how much of last period's revenue was kept from existing customers after accounting for churn and contraction but before any expansion. It is a ceiling metric no matter how good your expansion motion is, you cannot have NDR above your GDR. Net Dollar Retention adds expansion revenue back in on top of GDR. NDR above 100 percent is one of the most important signals in SaaS investing, because it means the existing customer base is growing on its own. A SaaS business with 120 percent NDR is structurally compounding. Investors typically want to see 110 to 120 percent NDR as a baseline for growth-stage SaaS companies, and anything above 120 percent is considered exceptional.

Burn Multiple and Magic Number

The Burn Multiple divides net cash burn by net new ARR over the same period. It answers the question every investor is now asking post-2022: for every dollar of new recurring revenue added, how many dollars of cash did it cost to get there? A burn multiple under 1 means the company is generating more new ARR than it is burning, which is outstanding. Above 2 starts to raise questions about whether the go-to-market is working efficiently. The Magic Number is a related metric that looks specifically at sales and marketing ROI, measuring how much net new ARR was generated for each dollar of S&M spend in the prior period. Both metrics are useful lenses on the same underlying question: is this company growing in a way that will eventually produce cash, or is it just buying revenue with investor capital?

Payback Period

Payback period tells you how many months of subscription gross margin it takes to cover the CAC for a new customer. If payback is 24 months, you need two years of a customer staying subscribed and paying before you break even on acquiring them, before any contribution to overhead or profit. For companies selling to SMBs, where churn is naturally higher, a 24-month payback is risky. For enterprise SaaS with multi-year contracts and high switching costs, it is more manageable. Most growth-stage SaaS investors consider 12 months a healthy payback range.

What Are the Different Types of SaaS Metrics and How Do They Differ by Level?

One of the most useful ways to organize SaaS metrics is to recognize that they operate at different levels and answer different questions.

Level 1
Unit Economics

At the unit economics level, the question is simple: is each individual customer relationship profitable, and by how much? This is where ARPA (Average Revenue Per Account), LTV, CAC, and payback period all live. In a SaaS business, every new customer starts as a money-losing relationship because the cost to acquire them typically exceeds their first month or even first year of subscription revenue. A SaaS company with 60 percent gross margins is in a very different unit economics position than one running at 80 percent, even if their ARR looks the same on the surface.

Level 2
Cohort Analysis

At the cohort level, you are zooming out from individual customers to groups of customers acquired during the same period, usually a month or quarter. This is where retention analysis really matters and where the difference between cohorted and uncohorted metrics is critical to understand. Uncohorted retention looks at aggregate churn across the whole customer base and is easy to misread. A SaaS company that is growing fast by adding lots of new customers can look like it has low churn on an uncohorted basis, even if older cohorts are leaking badly. The new customer volume masks the problem. Cohorted retention tracks what percentage of each acquisition cohort is still subscribed at specified periods this gives a much more honest view of whether the product is actually sticky.

Level 3
Business Efficiency

At the business efficiency level, metrics like Burn Multiple, Magic Number, and ARR Multiple answer a higher-level question: is the whole go-to-market machine working? Burn Multiple is the most widely used efficiency signal a catch-all metric that flags whether something is off without necessarily isolating what. A high burn multiple in a SaaS business could mean the sales team is spending too much on customer acquisition, it could mean gross margins are too low, or it could mean the company is in a full-build phase where heavy R&D spend is needed to compete and the burn will normalize once the product is complete. It needs to be paired with CAC, gross margin, and cohort retention to figure out which situation you are actually dealing with.

Level 4
Fund-Level Performance

At the fund level, the relevant numbers are IRR (Internal Rate of Return), TVPI (Total Value to Paid-In Capital), DPI (Distributions to Paid-In Capital), and RVPI (Residual Value to Paid-In). IRR is the annualized return on capital accounting for the timing of cash flows. TVPI is the total current value of the fund divided by total capital called, combining both what has been returned and what is still held. DPI specifically measures realized returns, meaning actual cash back to LPs, and is the metric that grows in importance as a fund matures. In early-stage SaaS-focused funds, DPI tends to sit near zero for the first several years, because SaaS exits take time to materialize. By year eight or nine, DPI is what LPs are watching most closely.

How Do Firms Think About Metrics Depending on Their Thesis and LP Commitments?

The metrics a firm emphasizes are directly shaped by their investment thesis, the stage they focus on, and the reporting commitments they have made to their LPs. A seed fund writing small checks into pre-product SaaS companies is working with almost no quantitative signal. A growth fund leading Series B rounds into companies with multiple millions in ARR is operating in a completely different information environment.

At the pre-seed and seed stage, the traditional SaaS metrics are often not there yet or are too noisy to be meaningful. A company with 15 customers does not have statistically reliable CAC or LTV numbers. What early-stage SaaS investors actually look at is more qualitative and directional. Are early customers actually using the product, measured by engagement metrics like DAU or session frequency? Are the handful of cohorts available showing any sign of flattening retention, even if the numbers are small? Is there any evidence of organic expansion within early accounts? These are weak signals, but they are the only signals available, and experienced seed investors have learned to read them carefully.

For growth-stage funds with a B2B SaaS thesis, the metrics framework becomes much more rigorous and the benchmarks get tighter fast. NDR moves from a nice-to-have signal to a dealbreaker. A growth fund evaluating a Series B SaaS opportunity will typically want to see NDR above 110 percent before they get comfortable with the retention story. They will look hard at cohorted retention at months 12 and 24, checking whether the retention curve is actually flattening or just decelerating its decline. Gross margin matters a lot at this stage too. SaaS companies should be targeting 75 to 80 percent gross margins at scale, and anything significantly below that raises questions about whether the business is genuinely software or whether it has a people-heavy delivery model hiding underneath the SaaS label. ARR growth rate expectations are also demanding for a competitive Series A or B round in SaaS, 2x to 3x year-over-year ARR growth is generally the baseline, not the stretch.

The investment thesis also directly shapes how firms weigh efficiency versus growth metrics. During the 2020 to 2022 period, when capital was cheap and exit multiples were high, many SaaS-focused funds were willing to overlook burn multiples above 3 as long as ARR growth was strong. Post-2022, when public SaaS multiples compressed dramatically and the fundraising environment tightened, that logic broke. LP sentiment shifted quickly toward capital efficiency. Funds revised their portfolio review criteria to weight burn multiple, payback period, and gross margin quality much more heavily.

LP clauses add a concrete layer of structure to all of this. Many Limited Partnership Agreements include specific reporting requirements tied to portfolio company metrics. Growth funds that have committed their LPs to quarterly reporting on NDR, ARR growth, and gross margin have a direct incentive to make sure their portfolio companies actually track and report those numbers in a consistent and auditable way. Some institutional LPs, particularly university endowments or fund-of-funds with their own reporting obligations, require both company-level metrics and fund-level performance reporting across IRR, TVPI, and DPI every quarter. This creates a real transmission belt from LP requirements to how GPs manage their portfolio.

There is also a real tension between portfolio-level SaaS metrics and fund-level performance metrics that does not get discussed enough. A fund with a thesis around capital-efficient B2B SaaS might have portfolio companies that look great on burn multiple and NDR but have not yet reached exit-scale. In that scenario, fund-level DPI stays near zero even as the underlying companies compound well. This is structurally normal for SaaS, where exit timelines tend to be longer than in consumer or marketplace businesses, but it creates friction with LPs who care about actual cash distributions. Funds that back longer-hold SaaS businesses need a clear and proactive narrative for their LPs about the exit pathway and timeline. Thesis clarity is not just about sourcing and due diligence in the LP relationship, it is what makes the reporting conversation make sense.

Product Metrics

What Are They and How Do They Evolve by Stage?

Product metrics are a different category from financial metrics they are the early warning signs that demonstrate if users are deriving value from the product.

Engagement

Engagement is measured at all stages of development as Daily Active Users, Weekly Active Users, and Monthly Active Users. For most B2B SaaS products, DAU and WAU are more useful than raw MAU, because they pick up on workflow integration and short-term retention. If a product is genuinely embedded in how a team works, people show up daily or at least every workday. MAU alone can be misleading, because a user who logs in once a month technically counts even if it is only once. The DAU to MAU ratio is more revealing of the utility of the product. For B2B SaaS, a DAU/MAU above 20 percent is generally considered healthy.

Pre-Seed and Seed

At pre-seed and seed stage, product metrics are often the only signal available. Investors look for evidence of an "aha" moment. Activation rate measures whether new users actually reach the point of experiencing the core value of the product completing onboarding, setting up an integration, creating their first meaningful output. A high activation rate, even with a small user base, suggests the product communicates its value quickly. Customer Satisfaction Score (CSAT) is also particularly useful at early stages, because it is a short-term signal of loyalty.

Early Growth

As a company moves into Series A and early growth stage, the product metric frame shifts from whether value exists to whether it compounds. Cohort retention curves become the primary tool. A healthy SaaS retention curve drops early as weak-fit users churn out, then flattens and stabilizes around a residual core of deeply engaged customers. That flattening state is what investors are looking at. A curve that never flattens and keeps declining is a sign that the product does not solve a true problem area or that switching costs are too low.

Growth and Expansion

At growth and expansion stage, the most important product metric becomes Net Promoter Score combined with feature adoption data. NPS is calculated by surveying users on how likely they are to recommend the product on a 10-point scale. Promoters score 9 or 10, passives 7 to 8, and detractors anything below. The calculation is the percentage of promoters minus the percentage of detractors. A high NPS predicts organic growth through referrals, which reduces CAC. It also predicts retention, because users who recommend a product are by definition loyal. For mature SaaS products, NPS scores above 40 are considered strong. Below 20, investor confidence in the retention story starts to waver. Feature adoption rate tracks what percentage of active users are engaging with a specific feature if adoption of a core workflow feature drops, NPS tends to follow, and that pattern shows up in churn data a few months later.

All Stages Time to Value

Time to Value (TTV) is demonstrated through all stages and becomes more important with scale. It measures how long it takes for a new user or account to experience meaningful value from the product after signing up. The faster a user gets to value, the more likely they are to stay and expand their usage or seat count. Product teams at growth-stage companies tend to obsess over shortening TTV because the effect compounds at scale. Shaving a day off onboarding time across thousands of new users per month has a measurable downstream impact on activation rates, early-period churn, and ultimately NDR.

What Are the Factors Integrated into SaaS Product Metrics?

It is important to understand the underlying factors in product metrics to be able to address the root of a customer pain point or lack of attractiveness.

Onboarding

Metrics such as activation rate, TTV, Day 7 and Day 30 retention, and early NPS are all indicative of how well the onboarding experience works. A user who reaches the "aha" moment within their first session is dramatically more likely to come back, as it shapes their first impression and models their perception of the product. This is especially true in B2B SaaS, where onboarding often involves configuring integrations, importing data, and setting up team permissions through legacy software. For startups, this configuration layer is itself a major pain point for large organizations as it requires employees to go through a full onboarding process. Products designed to address this friction are more likely to experience early wins better activation rates and that advantage carries forward through every downstream engagement metric.

Product-Market Fit

When fit is strong, users engage with the core workflow without being nudged. When fit is weak or partial, engagement tends to be episodic, NPS scores cluster in the passive range, and feature adoption data shows users only interacting with a narrow slice of functionality rather than going deeper over time. The Equals engagement framework distinguishes between surface engagement metrics like DAU and deeper quality engagement signals like A3x7, which measures how many users return to the product three or more times per week. A3x7 is harder to manipulate than DAU because a single marketing email can spike DAU without changing anything about how people actually use the product. A3x7 requires genuine habit formation.

Pricing and Packaging

There are two major types that dominate the SaaS market: usage-based and seat-based. Usage-based pricing creates a very direct link between product engagement and revenue. A customer who uses more features or processes more data pays more, and that alignment incentivizes customers to engage deeply. It also means engagement metrics become revenue forecasting inputs in a way they are not under flat seat-based pricing. Seat-based pricing, by contrast, can mask low engagement because the customer pays the same regardless of how much the product is used.

Customer Segment

Enterprises have structured onboarding processes, dedicated IT resources for implementations, and internal champions who enforce adoption. Their engagement patterns are more predictable and their retention curves typically flatten earlier and at a higher level. SMB customers have more volatile engagement as their priorities are constantly changing for business survival they are faster to adopt, but also faster to churn when budgets tighten. When a SaaS company shifts its ideal customer profile from enterprise to SMBs, they will typically see DAU/MAU ratios change, NPS improve, cohort retention curves flatten higher, and churn decline. This is simply a product of the customer segment changing and not the product improving.

Feature Breadth and Depth

A product with a narrow and deeply used feature set tends to generate strong engagement metrics on its core functionality, but can struggle to expand NPS or drive land-and-expand revenue because there is not enough for customers to grow into. A product with many features but shallow adoption of each tends to have high initial activation rates as users explore, followed by declining engagement as they settle into a narrow routine. Cohort adoption rate data reveals which features are genuinely sticky versus which ones get tried once and abandoned. This should feed directly into product roadmap decisions and inform investors on which pain point the company is really addressing. The highest-leverage investment is deepening the features people are already using rather than adding new ones nobody has gotten to yet.

How Do External Forces Affect SaaS Product Metrics and What Do the Changes Imply?

Some important external forces to consider are macro-economic conditions, competitive dynamics, platform changes, and technological shifts. Understanding how external forces move product metrics is important for interpreting metrics and for building a portfolio or company thesis around these forces.

Macro-Economic Conditions

In 2020 and 2021, remote work adoption accelerated demand for collaboration tools, project management software, and communication platforms. Usage metrics across the category surged as DAU/MAU ratios hit cycle highs. Churn rates dropped as companies rapidly embedded new tools into their workflows and enforced adoption. Then, as budget scrutiny returned after Covid, companies began auditing their SaaS stacks to cut redundant tools and renegotiating contracts. The effect showed up first in engagement metrics as fewer seats were renewed, then in churn data and MRR. ChartMogul's retention research from this period documented that the growth-at-all-costs approach of 2020 to 2021 left many SaaS companies with high volumes of poor-fit customers who were the first to churn when budgets tightened. The metric implication was clear: engagement and activation rates are only as meaningful as the quality of the customers they are measured across.

Recessions and Economic Downturns

Downturns hit SMB-facing SaaS especially hard SMBs cancel faster than enterprises, and their decision timelines are short. A wave of SMB cancellations shows up immediately in DAU as the lost accounts remove active users, then in NPS as remaining customers who feel budget-squeezed rate their overall experience lower. SaaS companies whose product metrics look strong in bull markets and deteriorate sharply in downturns reveal that their product-market fit is not appropriately targeted. Truly sticky products with high switching costs tend to see low churn in downturns because the cost of disruption to workflows is too high relative to the subscription fee.

Competitive Pressures

When a well-funded competitor launches, the first metric to move is typically NPS, particularly among detractors who now have a credible alternative. Churn follows NPS movement by three to six months on average, as the customers most frustrated with the incumbent product begin trialing and then committing to alternatives. Feature adoption data also shifts, as users who would previously have worked around product limitations start reducing engagement while they evaluate competitors. The implication for product teams is that NPS is a leading indicator of competitive churn risk, and monitoring NPS segmented by tenure is particularly valuable. Long-tenured customers who drop to detractor scores are often the ones conducting competitive evaluations and should trigger proactive outreach.

The Emergence of AI and Usage-Based Pricing

Traditional seat-based SaaS generated stable, predictable engagement patterns because every licensed seat was a potential active user and revenue was fixed regardless of usage. AI-native and usage-based products break this model. Revenue unfolds based on actual consumption, which means engagement metrics and revenue metrics become directly entangled. A spike in DAU now directly increases revenue, making ARR harder to predict from engagement signals because usage variability is high. NRR can appear large early on when usage is heaviest, then normalizes as usage routine emerges. For SaaS companies transitioning to usage-based models, product metrics need to be reframed around consumption intensity, expansion triggers, and usage milestones rather than simple active user counts.

Bain research from early 2026 highlighted that the reset in public SaaS valuations was partly driven by AI eroding incumbent SaaS vendors, as AI tools replicate core functionality at lower cost. This puts pressure on gross retention across categories where switching costs are low. Products with deep workflow integration, proprietary data advantages, or high configuration complexity tend to be more protected. Products that deliver relatively generic functionality on a SaaS delivery model are most exposed. The metric that separates them is product engagement depth specifically whether users are relying on the product for core workflows or just using it occasionally.

Platform Dependency

SaaS products built on top of major cloud providers or distribution platforms like Salesforce are subject to the strategic decisions of those platforms. When a platform changes its integration policies, deprecates an API, or launches a competing native feature, the SaaS startup can see DAU drop suddenly without any change in its own product quality. These large platforms will try to attract customers toward their own features to enable their land-and-expand strategy. Feature adoption metrics shift as users discover that a workflow they relied on through the SaaS tool is now available natively in the platform. NPS often suffers because users blame the SaaS vendor for disruption even when the cause is entirely external. The implication for portfolio companies that sit on top of large platforms is that product metric volatility from platform risk is real and needs to be priced into how investors interpret engagement trends. A DAU drop of 15 percent from a platform API change looks very different from a DAU drop driven by genuine product dissatisfaction, and distinguishing between the two requires tracking the external event alongside the internal metric.

Team Metrics

What Are They and How Do External Forces Shape Them?

The team is the only thing that actually carries those changes. Survey data consistently shows 95 percent of VCs rate the management team as a critical factor, with nearly half calling it the most important factor above product and market. Up to 65 percent of high-growth startup failures trace back to co-founder conflict and management dysfunction, not market timing or product quality.

Team metrics are signals gathered over time through conversations, references, and watching people under pressure. The main dimensions investors track are founder-market fit, team composition, execution history, coachability, and team stability. Each tells a different part of the story.

Founder-Market Fit

Founder-market fit is usually the first filter. The question is whether this specific person has a genuine and differentiated reason to build this specific company. A founder who spent a decade inside logistics software has a fundamentally different relationship to warehouse management problems than someone who researched it for a few months before starting. That lived experience shows up in conversations as deep knowledge of workflow pain points, buyer language, regulatory nuance, and competitive history. Paul Graham's essays on founder evaluation make the point that founders who are genuinely obsessed with a problem behave differently from those who found an idea that seemed commercially interesting. The obsessed ones keep building when commercial logic says they should stop.

Team Composition

Team composition addresses the team as a whole. Strong founding teams tend to cover technical depth, commercial execution, and product intuition without concentrating everything in one person. Garry Tan has written about six foundational skills teams need: engineering, product, sales, finance, management, and leadership. Rarely does one person carry all of these well. That is partly why solo founders face more skepticism and co-founder teams with genuine complementarity get more traction in diligence.

External Forces on Team Stability

External forces hit team metrics hard. Downturns stress-test team stability in ways that bull markets never do. When revenue slows, a fundraise falls through, or a co-founder relationship cracks under operational pressure, the team you thought you backed turns out to be a different one. Harvard Business Review research found that up to 43 percent of founders eventually buy out their co-founder.

Adaptability in the AI Era

The current AI disruption has also shifted which team qualities matter most. Deep domain expertise used to be nearly sufficient. Now, with software categories getting rebuilt in 12 to 18 month cycles, the ability to unlearn fast and adapt to new architectures is arguably more valuable than what a team already knows. Fred Wilson has written about how the founders and investors who navigate market transitions best are the ones who treat intellectual curiosity as an active practice rather than a settled state.

How Do You Identify a Great Team?

Great teams reveal themselves through the quality of their thinking, how they behave when something goes wrong, how they treat each other when they think nobody is watching, and what happens when a hard question punctures a comfortable narrative.

Founder-Market Fit as the First Filter

The real question is whether this specific person has a credible and differentiated reason to build this specific company. The VC Corner's framework for evaluating fit looks at five dimensions: relevant domain experience, personal connection to the problem, depth of industry knowledge, ability to attract talent in the space, and track record of learning from failure in adjacent areas. A founder who scores poorly on most of these is a different risk profile from one who has been living this problem for years. Without genuine fit, the path to product-market fit gets longer, early sales are harder, and the team tends to underestimate what they are actually taking on.

Leadership Over Management

Leadership matters more than management. This is a consistent point across Fred Wilson's AVC blog, Paul Graham's essays, and the GoingVC founder evaluation framework. Management skills are easier to learn. Vision is not. The form of leadership that predicts success in early-stage startups is closer to seeing where a market is going before it is obvious, articulating that vision compellingly enough that good people leave stable jobs to join, and holding the team's direction steady when the noise is loudest. The best founders update their tactical beliefs constantly, but the core insight about why the problem matters stays fixed.

Co-Founder Dynamics

Co-founder dynamics are one of the highest-signal areas of team evaluation and one of the easiest to miss in a formal pitch. Peter Thiel is known for asking co-founders how they met and how long they knew each other before starting. The logic is that teams who have navigated real conflict before starting tend to be more durable than teams assembled quickly around a shared idea. Watch how co-founders communicate during a pitch. Do they build on each other's points or does one go quiet when the other takes over? GoingVC has documented that VCs watch body language, conversational turn-taking, and how co-founders handle corrections from each other. These behavioral signals are hard to fake across a full diligence process.

Execution Track Record

Execution track record is worth excavating even in first-time founders. The best firms do not just read the resume. They look for what the founder actually built, how far it got, which decisions did not work, and how the founder talks about those failures now. A founder who can describe a past failure with clinical honesty, naming specifically what went wrong and what they would do differently, has demonstrated self-awareness and intellectual courage. A founder who frames every past setback as market conditions or someone else's fault is signaling something important about how they will process the next failure too.

Hiring Quality

Hiring quality is underrated as a team signal. Sifted's research found that outlier founders hired 25 to 30 VP and C-suite executives in the first five years, most at significant salary levels. The ability to attract strong talent is itself a measure of the founding team's reputation, network, and ability to sell a vision. Who has already joined and why tends to tell you a lot about how founders are perceived by the people who know them best.

What to Look For and What Strategies Get an Insider View?

Knowing what a great team looks like is one thing. Finding reliable ways to verify those qualities before committing capital is a different discipline. The formal pitch is designed to present founders at their best. Real diligence finds ways to observe them in contexts they did not prepare for.

The five qualities that consistently come up across top evaluation frameworks are resilience under pressure, clarity of thought, genuine curiosity, coachability, and integrity. Resilience is not the absence of visible stress it means decision-making quality does not collapse when things go badly. Clarity of thought is assessed by how founders explain complex things simply. Curiosity shows up in how founders engage with ideas outside their domain and how fast they update when they get new information. Coachability is tested directly by challenging something the founder said and watching whether they engage or get defensive. Integrity is evaluated through consistency across settings, whether the story about the business sounds the same when talking to investors, customers, and their own team.

Back-Channel References

Back-channel references are where the real information lives. The standard approach of calling three founder-provided references yields almost nothing useful. Those people were selected and prepared. The better move is reaching out to people who know the founder but were not put forward: former colleagues, people who worked under them, people who competed against them, early employees who left. The questions that produce the most useful signals are not about competence but about character. How does this person behave when things go wrong? Do they take accountability or assign blame? Would you work with them again if you had a choice? Use at least four to six references and prioritize those who have seen the founder navigate genuine adversity.

The Unscripted Follow-Up

The meeting after the formal pitch matters as much as the pitch itself. On the 20VC podcast, a recurring theme is that elite investors schedule unscripted follow-up conversations and watch how founders use the time. Do they show up with new thinking? Do they ask thoughtful questions about the investor's portfolio? Or do they treat it as another sales meeting and run through the same material again? The unscripted conversation reveals how founders actually think versus how they present.

Working Sessions

Working sessions are becoming more common. Some investors ask founding teams to walk through a live product decision, a hiring dilemma they are currently navigating, or a customer situation that went badly. Sifted's reporting on European VC diligence documented that firms like EQT Ventures and Digital+ Partners have moved toward embedding staff in the diligence process specifically to observe team dynamics across multiple touchpoints rather than relying on a single partner's read from one meeting. Northzone uses a structured framework that tracks how founders communicate under questioning across multiple sessions, looking for consistency in how they describe challenges and how they interact with each other when things get uncomfortable.

Customer Conversations

Investors who talk to early customers not just to validate product-market fit but to understand how the founding team operates in the field get a different kind of information. Customers who worked closely with the team during implementation can describe how founders handled problems when the product broke, how quickly they responded, and whether they were honest about limitations or oversold. A team that is straightforward with customers when something fails is usually the same team that will be straightforward with investors. That consistency is hard to fake across multiple independently gathered data points.

Long-Form Founder Interviews

Listen to long-form founder interviews. How I Built This with Guy Raz has compiled hundreds of candid founder stories over a decade. The best ones reveal something specific: founders who talk about failure with the same energy as success, who can name the exact moment they almost quit and explain what kept them going, and who describe their co-founder relationships with the same candor they bring to everything else. That pattern of authentic storytelling trains your ear for what genuine conviction sounds like which makes it easier to recognize when a pitch room narrative feels constructed versus lived. That distinction is one of the most important calls an investor has to make.

Market Analysis

How Do You Evaluate New Innovations in a Market Where a Startup Operates?

It starts with the market, as it is important to understand what the future state of the industry will look like. A16z's 2015 framework for startup metrics makes the point that market evaluation has to account for where the TAM is going, not just where it is today. A market at $500M growing at 40% annually is a more interesting investment context than one at $5B growing at 3%. The key question is whether the innovation being evaluated is driving that growth trajectory or just riding it. A company that is the cause of market expansion has structural pricing power and category definition advantages. A company that is just riding a trend is vulnerable the moment a better-positioned rider shows up.

Market's Growth Potential

Mike Vernal of Sequoia wrote about the market curve specifically to push back on the instinct to evaluate markets as static things. The best companies create the market they then capture. When evaluating a startup's innovation, the right question is not just whether the market exists but whether this product is doing something that could make the market bigger than it currently appears. That lens changes how you weight early traction numbers. A company with $2M ARR in a market it is actively creating looks very different from a company with $2M ARR chasing a mature TAM dominated by incumbents.

Engagement

If the innovation is genuinely solving a pain point, early users do not need to be nudged to engage. Activation rates will be high organically and time-to-value will be short. Cohort retention curves should show the characteristic early drop followed by a flattening, which signals real product-market fit rather than curiosity-driven trial. ICONIQ's SaaS Glossary treats NRR above 110% as a baseline signal of genuine value creation, because customers who expand usage over time are voting with budget.

Burn Multiple

The Andreessen Horowitz 16 Startup Metrics piece makes the case that burn multiple is not just a financial metric. For innovation evaluation it is a proxy for conviction. A company burning $3 to generate $1 of new ARR is not an innovation story it is an acquisition story dressed up as one. The arithmetic of real innovation should eventually reduce that ratio as distribution costs fall and the product's reputation does the selling. Watching burn multiple trends over three to four quarters tells you whether the market is opening up or just getting more expensive to develop.

How Might Consumers React to a Startup Changing the Market?

Understanding how consumers actually respond to disruption is one of the more underrated parts of evaluating a startup, and the evidence for what works is pretty clear.

Time to Normalization

The dominant pattern is initial resistance followed by rapid normalization once a tipping point is crossed. David Skok's SaaS Metrics 2.0 framework documents this in terms of payback period and churn dynamics. In the early stages of a new product entering a market, the customers who do adopt are high-engagement early adopters and churn looks deceptively low because the cohort self-selected. The real consumer reaction test comes in months 12 to 18, when early adopters have been through real usage cycles and the mainstream user base starts encountering the product. That is when you see the retention curve either stabilize or deteriorate. A curve that flattens signals that normal consumers are finding stable value. One that keeps declining tells you the adoption was wider than the value proposition.

Pricing

Metronome's 2025 State of Usage-Based Pricing report found that usage-based models deliver 22% lower churn and 10% higher NRR compared to flat-rate alternatives. The mechanism is alignment. When customers pay based on value consumed, they self-select into the level of usage that actually works for them, and they are less likely to cancel a subscription they feel they are actively benefiting from. A startup that changes market pricing norms moving from per-seat to consumption is not just making a product decision, it is making a consumer psychology decision, and the retention data suggests consumers respond to that alignment positively over time.

Adoption Curve

SMB and enterprise consumers react very differently to market disruption. SMBs adopt fast but churn fast too. High early NPS with deteriorating 12-month retention often means the product found real early-stage enthusiasm but is not sticky enough to survive budget reviews. Enterprise consumers are slower to adopt the evaluation cycle is longer and involves more stakeholders. Vena Solutions research found an average of five decision-makers in a SaaS purchase with finance involved 44% of the time. But once they are in, they tend to stay. A startup that can navigate that enterprise adoption curve has a fundamentally different consumer response profile than one optimized for SMB volume.

Engagement Depth

Where startups most commonly misread consumer reaction is in confusing sign-up with adoption and adoption with value. The ChartMogul 2024 SaaS Retention Report established that even top-quartile companies at $15M to $30M ARR are struggling to maintain NRR above 100%. Consumer response has evolved startups need to earn consumer permanence. The metrics that separate real adoption from surface-level usage are engagement depth and cohort retention.

How Do You Identify and Weight Market Headwinds and Tailwinds?

Every startup operates inside forces it did not create and cannot fully control. Being able to identify whether those forces are working for or against the company and how much weight to put on each is one of the most consequential and least systematized parts of early-stage investing.

Tailwinds

Tailwinds create external conditions that accelerate the adoption of a specific product. In 2020, Covid accelerated demand for collaboration tools that benefited their DAU/MAU ratios and NRR numbers. Battery Ventures' State of the Open Cloud tracks this kind of structural tailwind across cloud categories, and the data is consistent: companies whose growth is partly explained by a macro tailwind tend to show NRR above 115% during the expansion phase of that tailwind, and reversion toward 100% or below when the force normalizes. Identifying whether a tailwind is structural and durable or cyclical and temporary is the thing most investors get wrong.

Headwinds

Headwinds are the inverse and usually more complex to model. Bain's early 2026 analysis of the public SaaS selloff documented that AI tools replicating core functionality at lower cost are now creating systematic headwinds for categories where switching costs have historically been low. A cyclical headwind like rising interest rates affects valuations and fundraising, but does not usually change the underlying unit economics of a working product. A structural headwind like competitive AI substitution can erode gross retention permanently, because the product is now being compared to something cheaper that did not exist two years ago. These require very different responses from both founders and investors.

A startup with a strong tailwind from AI adoption but a real headwind from platform dependency risk built on top of a single cloud provider who could replicate their functionality natively is not just netting out to neutral. The platform headwind can terminate the tailwind in a single product decision by the incumbent. Fred Wilson's writing on investment pace at AVC makes the point that markets change faster than portfolios can be repositioned, and the firms that survive transitions are the ones who had already identified where the structural risks lived and built their positions accordingly.

Categorizing by Duration and Magnitude

The practical approach is to categorize forces by duration and magnitude. A tailwind that is regulatory, like the shift to digital health records, has a longer and more predictable arc than one that is cultural, like consumer preference for privacy. A headwind that is structural, like AI substitution of low-differentiation SaaS, is more dangerous than one that is cyclical, like the current enterprise budget freeze. Mostly Metrics makes the point that SaaS benchmarking across cohorts gives you the base rates that let you distinguish a company actually outperforming headwinds from one that is just being carried by tailwinds and has not been tested yet. Both look fine on a single ARR chart. They look very different on a burn-adjusted, cohort-tracked retention curve when the tailwind stops.

Competition Metrics

How Do You Find Competition in SaaS?

A founder who says they have no competition is either lying or hasn't looked hard enough. Both are problematic behaviors and perceptions that VCs are aware of. First Round Capital has said explicitly that partner meetings often involve competitive analysis that founders did not expect, because the investors have already spoken to customers, researched adjacent markets, and sometimes taken pitches from the exact companies the founder claims don't exist.

Platforms

The obvious starting point is G2 and review platforms. For B2B SaaS, this platform has real customer sentiment, in-depth feature comparisons, and user ratings across competing products. Gartner's Magic Quadrant maps vendors on ability to execute and completeness of vision, making it the standard reference for more established categories. It compares Leaders, Challengers, Visionaries, and Niche Players and gives a structured view of who the market believes is winning and why. For earlier markets, Forrester Wave reports and the Gartner Hype Cycle are appropriate alternatives.

Customer Feedback

Beyond the obvious tools, the deeper competitive intelligence work involves talking to customers. This is the most underused source and also the most valuable. Customers who have evaluated multiple vendors in a category can describe competitor positioning, pricing, weaknesses, and sales tactics in more detail than any analyst report. First Round's diligence process typically involves five to fifteen calls with actual or prospective customers precisely because these conversations surface competitive intelligence that would take months to find any other way. A founder who has done this work first, and can speak fluently about how their best prospects evaluated competitors before choosing them, shows a level of market understanding that pattern-matching investors respond to.

Job Postings

Job postings are another underrated signal. Competitors hiring aggressively in a specific function tell the founder where they are investing. LinkedIn is useful for tracking executive moves which can signal product pivots or new market pushes. Crunchbase and PitchBook show who got funded recently and at what valuation, which signals where capital believes the competition is heading. Glassdoor can reveal operational dysfunction inside a competing firm. The already.dev competitive intelligence blog notes that VCs will ask specifically how the competitive landscape has changed in the last six months, which is a test of whether a founder is monitoring continuously or just doing point-in-time analysis before a fundraise. It is important to note that these platforms have delayed information access if information is available, it is already a few weeks after the event has happened. The companies that treat competitive intelligence as a habit rather than a project tend to be the ones whose market maps actually hold up under questioning.

What Do You Look for When Evaluating Competition?

How a founder explains the competition demonstrates their understanding of the market, their risks, and whether the positioning they have chosen is defensible. A slide with four boxes where the company sits alone in the top right corner is a warning sign about the founder's self-awareness.

Types of Competitors

In SaaS, competition comes in three forms. Direct competitors are building the same product for the same customer. Indirect competitors are solving the same problem with a different approach, which often includes spreadsheets, manual processes, or internal tools. And platform risk competitors are the large incumbents Salesforce, Microsoft, SAP who could bundle the functionality into an existing product and sell it to the same buyers at zero marginal cost. David Haber wrote in the a16z Big Ideas 2025 piece that differentiation builds a moat, but differentiation and defensibility are distinct. Confusing the two presents a risk of being outflanked by more strategic competitors.

Switching Costs

How hard is it for a customer who has adopted a competitor's product to leave? In SaaS this comes from data migration friction, workflow integration depth, team retraining costs, and contractual lock-in. Greylock's essay on the new moats makes the point that traditional SaaS switching costs retraining teams, migrating data, enduring downtime remain relevant but are no longer sufficient on their own. The question is no longer just whether switching is painful but whether the product is building compounding value that makes the customer better off over time regardless of switching costs. Products that only retain customers through friction are fragile. Products that retain customers because they keep getting more valuable are structurally different.

Strongest Competitors

Which competitor worries a VC most and why? The already.dev blog documents that this is one of the standard questions VCs ask, and they are explicitly looking for honesty and self-awareness over bravado. A founder who can name a competitor, explain specifically what that company does well, articulate where the risk of losing to them is highest, and then explain what the defensive play is, signals market depth. A founder who dismisses every competitor as inferior or misguided signals the opposite. Investors have usually taken pitches from those competitors. They know the story from both sides.

AI Substitution

Bain's early 2026 analysis of the public SaaS reset flagged that AI tools replicating core functionality at lower cost are putting real pressure on gross retention in categories where switching costs have historically been low. For investors evaluating competition now, the question is not just who is in the market today but whether an AI-native entrant could replicate the core value proposition in 18 months at a fraction of the cost. Products with proprietary data, deep workflow integration, and high configuration complexity are more protected. Generic SaaS on commodity delivery is most exposed. Greylock's updated moat framework concludes that data network effects have become the singularly most important characteristic in AI-era competition, ahead of what were previously the top two defensible characteristics: scale economies and switching costs.

What Differentiation Is Important for SaaS?

Differentiation in SaaS is not about having a better feature set those get copied and roadmaps converge. The question investors are actually asking when they evaluate differentiation is whether this company has built something that structurally compounds over time, in a way that makes it progressively harder for a competitor to replicate. The vocabulary for this is moats, and different types of moats matter differently at different stages of a SaaS company's life.

Workflow Integration

Workflow integration is one of the most durable forms of differentiation for B2B SaaS and one of the most underappreciated. When a product becomes genuinely embedded in how a team works, switching is not just costly in terms of data migration it is costly in terms of process disruption, retraining, and loss of institutional memory stored inside the product. Palantir is the extreme version of this. The implementation cost is high enough to be a barrier to sale, but once in, switching is nearly impossible. Most SaaS companies do not have that level of integration depth, but the principle applies at lower levels. Products that own a daily workflow that the team opens first thing every morning and uses throughout the day have a fundamentally different retention profile from products that are used episodically or only by power users.

Data Network Effects

Data network effects are becoming the most important form of differentiation in AI-era SaaS. Oxx VC's essay on moats in AI argues that data network effects have moved to the top of the defensibility stack, above switching costs and scale economies, precisely because AI systems improve as they learn from more usage. A legal AI tool trained on five years of proprietary contract data from actual law firm workflows is in a different competitive position from one built on publicly available legal text. The more customers use the product, the more accurate the model gets and the more value it creates compared to competitors. This is a compounding dynamic that is genuinely hard to replicate, and it means early entry into a data-rich vertical has a structural advantage that capital cannot easily replicate.

Traditional Network Effects

Network effects at the platform and ecosystem level are a third category. Greylock's updated moat essay distinguishes between direct network effects, where each new user directly improves the product for existing users, and indirect effects, where the ecosystem around the platform grows in a way that adds value to the core product. Salesforce's AppExchange is the clearest B2B example. The more apps built on the platform, the more valuable the platform becomes for every enterprise customer, creating a flywheel that pure CRM functionality alone could not sustain. For a SaaS startup thinking about differentiation, the question is whether the product has any design patterns that create this kind of ecosystem lock-in, or whether it is a standalone point solution with no particular reason for the ecosystem to form around it.

Pricing

Pricing as a metric is undervalued customers shift to the lower-priced product in recessions, and those are the businesses that survive long term. The shift from seat-based to usage-based pricing changes the competitive dynamics of the category. A competitor who charges flat per-seat fees is structurally disadvantaged against a product that scales pricing with value delivered. The usage-based model can offer a lower entry price to gain the customer's trust, then reach full scale to generate more revenue from high-engagement customers. Metronome's 2025 State of Usage-Based Pricing report found that usage-based models deliver 10 percent higher NRR, 22 percent lower churn, and two times faster growth. Those outcomes are in part a function of strong pricing alignment when price directly represents value, the customer relationship is structurally different.

The a16z framing on differentiation versus defensibility is worth sitting with. Differentiation solving a wedge problem ten times better than anything else in the market earns the founder the right to build a relationship with customers. It is how the founder gets in the door. Defensibility is what keeps competitors out. A lot of SaaS companies do the first part right and neglect the second. They build a genuinely better product, win early customers on product merit, and then get surprised three years later when a better-funded competitor copies the core feature set and competes on distribution. VCs want to know what makes the product better today and what makes the company harder to beat in two to seven years.

Growth Potential

What Are the Growth Metrics and What Influences Them?

Growth metrics are the quantitative signals investors use to assess whether a SaaS company is scaling efficiently and sustainably. Unlike vanity metrics, these figures reflect the underlying health of a business model and its ability to generate recurring value over time.

ARR Growth Rate

Annual Recurring Revenue growth rate is the most fundamental indicator of a SaaS company's trajectory. Investors look for consistent, compounding growth typically 100%+ year-over-year for early-stage companies as evidence of strong product-market fit and expanding demand. ARR growth is influenced by new customer acquisition, expansion revenue from existing accounts, and churn reduction.

Net Dollar Retention (NDR)

NDR measures revenue retained from existing customers over a period, including expansions, upgrades, and contractions. A NDR above 120% indicates that a company grows its revenue base even without acquiring new customers a hallmark of best-in-class SaaS businesses. NDR is driven by product stickiness, upsell motion, and the degree to which customers are realizing ongoing value.

Burn Multiple

The Burn Multiple, calculated as net burn divided by net new ARR, measures how efficiently a company converts capital into growth. A ratio below 1x is considered exceptional; above 2x signals inefficiency. This metric is heavily influenced by go-to-market costs, hiring pace, and the stage of the company's growth journey.

CAC Payback Period

The Customer Acquisition Cost payback period represents the number of months required to recover the cost of acquiring a customer. Industry benchmarks vary by segment, but sub-18-month payback is generally considered strong for enterprise SaaS. This metric is shaped by sales cycle length, contract size, and the efficiency of marketing spend.

Magic Number

The Magic Number gauges go-to-market efficiency by comparing net new ARR to prior-quarter sales and marketing spend. A Magic Number above 0.75 is viewed favorably, suggesting that increased investment in growth will yield proportional returns. External factors such as market saturation, competitive intensity, and sales team maturity all shape this figure.

How to Enhance the Growth Potential of a Startup?

Improving growth metrics is not solely the function of capital deployment it requires deliberate operational and strategic decisions that compound over time.

Expand Into Larger Markets

A startup's growth ceiling is often determined by its initial market definition. Companies that proactively identify adjacent verticals, move upmarket into enterprise segments, or expand geographically can sustain higher ARR growth rates as core markets mature. Founders should model total addressable market expansions and build product capabilities that support these transitions before the core market flattens.

Deepen Product Stickiness

Net Dollar Retention improves when customers integrate deeply into the product. Strategies include building workflow automation that becomes central to daily operations, creating data network effects where the product becomes more valuable the longer a customer uses it, and developing integrations that embed the product into existing enterprise stacks. The harder the product is to remove, the lower the churn and the higher the expansion opportunity.

Improve Go-to-Market Efficiency

Burn Multiple and CAC Payback Period improve when growth is generated with less friction. This involves refining ideal customer profiles, tightening sales qualification, investing in product-led growth motions that reduce reliance on high-cost outbound sales, and building repeatable playbooks that scale without proportional headcount increases.

How to Provide Help as a VC Investor?

Venture investors can meaningfully accelerate growth metrics through portfolio support that goes beyond capital. The most valuable contributions are those that reduce friction in the company's path to scale.

Customer Introductions

Warm introductions to potential enterprise customers through the investor's network can compress sales cycles and improve CAC Payback Period by shortening the time to first dollar of revenue. Investors with deep relationships in the target verticals of their portfolio companies provide outsized go-to-market leverage.

Recruiting Support

Growth metrics are ultimately driven by the quality of the team executing against them. Investors who actively source and refer senior sales, marketing, and product talent help portfolio companies build the organizational capacity to sustain high ARR growth rates without disproportionate increases in burn.

Benchmarking and Data Access

Investors with broad portfolio visibility can provide proprietary benchmarks on NDR, Magic Number, and CAC Payback by stage and vertical context that is difficult for early-stage founders to access independently. Sharing this data helps leadership teams calibrate performance against peers and make more informed resource allocation decisions.

Follow-On Capital Reserves

Investors who reserve capital for follow-on rounds reduce dilution pressure on founders and ensure that companies with strong growth momentum can continue to invest in go-to-market expansion without being forced into suboptimal fundraising timelines. Signaling follow-on commitment also strengthens a company's position with co-investors in future rounds.

Intersection of Hardware & Software

What is Hardware-Aware Software?

Hardware-aware software refers to the systems embedded in robotics. This adds additional layers of complexity for the software components as it must close the loop between understanding the task and orchestrating hardware to execute an action in real time. This requires a deeper and more complex edge of the network without cloud connectivity. For such products, the hardware and software must be built simultaneously. The algorithm is co-designed with the silicon, sensors, and actuators it runs on.

The market is moving very progressively in that direction as there are tailwinds driving it forward.

  • Hardware costs are falling fast. The biggest challenge used to be the OpEx of the components. Now, with off-the-shelf robotics available and efficient manufacturing, it is becoming progressively accessible. Goldman Sachs documented a 40 percent drop in humanoid robot manufacturing costs between 2023 and 2024.
  • Vision-language-action models have heavily evolved and foundation models can generalize across tasks and environments. This creates a new layer of efficiency in development as it removes the need to reprogram robots for each new scenario.
  • Edge AI chips are cutting inference latency by up to 60 percent, enabling autonomous operation in unstructured settings.

This software for the hardware market has exponentially grown in the last year and it is continuously growing.

What Are Some Products in Development?

Physical Intelligence (Pi) San Francisco

Pi builds general-purpose foundation models for robotics. Its pi-zero model, a three-billion-parameter vision-language-action system, was trained across seven robot embodiments and 68 tasks. The follow-on model, pi-zero-point-five, generalized to entirely new homes and offices without prior training in those settings. Pi has raised $1.1 billion in total and reached a valuation of $5.6 billion in its November 2025 Series B, backed by Alphabet's CapitalG, Sequoia, and NVIDIA.

Mecka AI Toronto

Mecka builds the data layer for physical AI, collecting and labeling egocentric human video to train humanoid robots. Founded in 2024 by Josh Gao, Jason Chong, Duy Nguyen, and Mogen Cheng, the company hires baristas, mechanics, and cooks to film themselves performing everyday tasks, then processes and anonymizes that footage into labeled datasets that robotics companies can feed directly into their models. Home robotics company 1X Technologies is an early customer. Mecka raised an $8 million seed round in August 2025, led by NEO, with participation from SV Angel, A-Star Capital, Cade Ventures, Timeless, and Offline VC.

GrayMatter Robotics Carson, California

GrayMatter deploys physical AI systems for high-variability manufacturing tasks such as surface preparation, coating, and inspection. It has processed over 30 million square feet of surface area across 20-plus states. In April 2026, GrayMatter signed a memorandum of understanding with HII, America's largest shipbuilder, to automate naval fabrication processes. HII's EVP noted that physical AI unlocks a new category in which a single robot performs 100,000 different tasks, each only once.

What Experience Does a Founding Team Need?

Talent at the intersection of robotics, machine learning, and systems software is the primary growth constraint in this space. Founding teams that succeed consistently combine four types of experience:

  • Domain depth in robotics, control systems, or embedded hardware at a production level not just academic familiarity.
  • AI and machine learning expertise specifically in perception, planning, or real-world reinforcement learning.
  • Systems integration experience from prior industry deployments, because the gap between a laboratory prototype and a production system is large.
  • A commercial operator who can translate technical capability into customer contracts, supply chain relationships, and investor narratives.

Physical Intelligence illustrates this directly. Its technical co-founders include former Google DeepMind researchers and Stanford and Berkeley professors. Its COO is Lachy Groom, a former Stripe product leader who manages fundraising and go-to-market. Prior experience in regulated industries such as defense, medical devices, and aerospace is increasingly viewed as a differentiator by investors as they know how to navigate the parts with the most barriers.

What is the Growth Potential?

Physical AI is believed to be strongly correlated with the potential growth of hardware-aware software. As global labor shortfalls exceed 50 million workers, and wages are rising in manufacturing, logistics, and healthcare, this creates persistent demand for automation. Physical AI is the first technology capable of addressing this at scale, because it can generalize across tasks rather than requiring custom programming for each one. This growth is further evidenced as industrial robots are projected to grow at a CAGR of 56.7 percent through 2032, and service robots are expanding at 40.7 percent annually.

The growth of hardware is creating a software flywheel that compounds. Each robot requires hardware-aware software to deploy. Once deployed, it generates real-world data that improves foundation models, which subsequently makes more complex deployments feasible and accelerates further adoption. This compounding advantage does not exist in traditional robotics.

What Does the Future Outlook Look Like?

Several clear trajectories are shaping the next phase of this space:

  • Consumer markets are opening. Deloitte's 2026 Tech Trends report identifies household robotics including elder care, cleaning, and meal preparation as the large long-term opportunity beyond industrial applications.
  • Regulation will shape adoption curves. A high-profile incident involving an autonomous robot could trigger restrictive responses across entire sectors. Companies investing early in safety architectures and regulatory engagement are building a durable moat.
  • Geopolitical fragmentation is a structural risk. The San Francisco–Shenzhen technology divide is creating pressure toward incompatible regional ecosystems in platforms, chips, and data standards.
  • Commoditization will drive generalization. Open-source models such as Lerobot and OpenVLA are compressing margins on lower-stack components. However, companies with proprietary training data, hardware, or deep vertical integration into regulated industries are best positioned their information asymmetry and specialization will act as their main moat and keep customers loyal.

The most fundamental shift is in how products are defined. A robot will increasingly be evaluated on the intelligence of its behavior, which is a function of training data and foundational software. Hardware becomes secondary as the learned model becomes the product.

Changes in the UX/UI Landscape

What is the Overview on the Space?

The way the world interacts with software has completely shifted after mass adoption of AI. There used to be a gap between what was imagined and what was built. That gap cost time, introduced errors, and meant that iteration was slow. The AI wave that started reaching product teams is replacing iteration phases entirely.

In 2011, Marc Andreessen wrote that software was "eating" the world. This has shifted: AI is now eating the interface between humans and software. UX and UI is increasingly becoming a prompt, a natural language instruction, or a conversation. The downstream effects of that shift are landing across every industry that runs on software.

First Round Capital has written about the compounding advantage that comes from shortening the feedback loop between idea and working product. When that loop goes from weeks to hours, the number of experiments a team can run per quarter multiplies exponentially, and the quality of decisions made from those experiments compounds over time. That is the structural change the new UX/UI tooling is enabling, and it is why the investment dollars are following.

What Products Are in Development in the Space?

The products being built in this space follow a similar thesis: the friction between deciding what to build and actually building it is the single most expensive cost in product development, and it can be dramatically reduced with AI. Three distinct categories have emerged.

Dessn, London UK

Design-to-production tooling is where the output of a design process becomes deployable code without a handoff. Dessn, a London-based startup founded in 2024, connects directly to a team's GitHub repository, extracts the existing component library, tokens, and design system, and then builds a visual environment around the real codebase. A designer or product manager can prototype features directly in the context of what is already in production. The resulting code is a pull request that goes to the developer for review. Dessn's co-founder Gabriella Hachem, who has a background spanning product, design, and engineering at Planned and Salesfloor, described the product as collapsing the silos between environments that have historically operated in completely different worlds. It eliminates the translation layer between design and engineering that has been responsible for the majority of implementation drift in product teams.

Replit, Foster City

Natural language app creation is where people can build functional software without a technical background. Replit, founded in 2016 and based in Foster City, California, has become the defining company in this space. Its agents allow a user to describe what they want to build in plain language and receive a working and deployed application. The company reported $240 million in revenue for 2025 and targets $1 billion in 2026. Teams at Zillow, Duolingo, and Coinbase use it, and 85 percent of Fortune 500 companies have teams building on the platform. This demonstrates that the need to build easier deployable applications runs across individuals as well as the world's largest companies. For example, Talkdesk, valued at $10 billion, used Replit to build a headcount capacity app in two days that would have previously taken two weeks.

General Magic, Toronto

The third category is conversational interface layers applied to legacy industry workflows. General Magic builds SMS-native AI agents for the insurance industry. Its product, Cell, handles the entire customer lifecycle over text: pre-quote eligibility, post-quote follow-up, document collection, and claims coordination. In early deployments with one of the world's largest general insurers, it reduced time-to-quote from roughly 30 minutes to under three minutes and cut inbound call volume by 30 percent. The company raised a $7.2 million seed led by Radical Ventures, with participation from a16z Speedrun, whose investment partner Troy Kirwin described the founders as building a product that will revolutionize workflows across insurance carriers and brokerages globally.

What Do Founding Teams Need and How Do They Discover the Problem?

The experience profile that consistently predicts success in this space is someone who has felt the friction personally. Dessn's Gabriella Hachem came from exactly that background, having operated across product, design, and engineering before building a tool to remove the handoff problem she experienced firsthand. General Magic's founders pivoted after a personal encounter with insurance's broken customer experience: a water leak that spiralled into weeks of calls, delays, and higher premiums became the founding insight. Replit's Amjad Masad, a former Facebook engineer, built an in-browser coding environment because he had seen how technical complexity kept non-technical people on the sidelines of software creation.

What all three of these founding stories share is a discovery process that was user-centric before it was product-centric. First Round Capital's writing on founder evaluation emphasizes that the teams who build the most durable products are those who spent real time close to the problem in practice, not just in research. The discovery phase in UX/UI-native companies requires an unusually deep understanding of workflow psychology. The thesis revolves around the environment in which the frictions lived. People drop off during insurance quoting since the process was long and confusing. Designers and engineers miscommunicate during handoff because they are working in environments that do not share a common language. The best founding teams in this space have spent enough time inside those workflows to understand the emotional texture of the problem, not just the functional one.

In practice, this means the discovery phase should involve direct observation and co-creation with users before any product decisions are made. The risk in UX tooling specifically is that founders build for their own workflow preferences rather than for the actual range of users who will adopt the product. Replit addressed this by designing explicitly for non-technical users, which kept the interface intuitive rather than powerful-but-complicated. General Magic addressed it by embedding regulatory and licensing compliance into the agent's behavior from the start, because they learned through customer conversations that insurance workflows have compliance requirements that generic AI communication tools cannot handle.

What is the Growth Potential of This Industry?

The growth potential depends on three structural tailwinds that compound together.

Access Increasing

The first is the expansion of who can build software. Replit's thesis is that one billion people will eventually create software. This was proven as the company's revenue went from $2.8 million annualized in early 2024 to $240 million by end of 2025. The 85x year-over-year multiple was driven entirely by making software creation accessible to non-technical users. That growth rate is not a company-specific anomaly. It is a signal about the size of the latent demand that existed before the product existed.

Cost Decreasing

The second tailwind is the cost of iteration. When a product team can transform an idea into a working prototype in hours, they are able to run more experiments, learn faster, and allocate resources more accurately. Battery Ventures' State of the OpenCloud research documents that companies with higher iteration velocity consistently produce better unit economics over time, because they reach product-market fit with less capital. The UX/UI tooling layer is infrastructure for iteration velocity, and the companies building it are selling to every software-building organization in the world.

AI Penetration into Legacy Industries

The third tailwind is vertical AI penetration into legacy industries. General Magic has a thesis that insurance can run over text messages. It is a replication of what happened when email replaced fax, and when mobile banking replaced branch visits. Every industry that has a high-friction, low-satisfaction customer interaction pattern is a candidate for a conversational AI interface. Radical Ventures partner Sanjana Basu put it precisely: most of the world's financial and insurance data is locked inside rigid legacy systems that were never designed for the AI era. The companies that build the reasoning layer on top of those systems are not solving a software design problem, they are solving an access problem, and access problems at scale produce very large businesses.

What Does the Future Outlook Look Like as the Space Evolves?

The most interesting product decisions in UX and UI over the next five years will be about how much of the interface can be removed entirely and replaced by a model that understands what the user is trying to do without requiring them to navigate to it. General Magic's SMS interface for insurance is an early version of this. There is no app to download, no portal to log into, no menu to navigate. The customer sends a text and the system handles the rest.

Consumer preferences are accelerating in this direction. Replit's mobile agent, which allows users to describe an app and have it deployed to the App Store in days, is not a developer tool. It is a consumer product that happens to produce software. As the generation of users who grew up with conversational AI interfaces enters the workforce and the consumer economy, the expectation that software should respond to intent rather than require navigation will become the default assumption.

The durable advantage will be built on workflow depth, regulatory compliance integration, and data that accumulates with every user interaction and makes the system progressively better at predicting and serving the next user. The companies that understand they are building infrastructure for how work gets done are the ones positioned to define the next decade of this space.

Therefore, it is crucial for operators to do a macro analysis on major market and policy changes as well as monitoring the NRR. If retention weakens, it is a clear signal that this is likely a company and product driven change.

Net Revenue Retention (NRR)

NRR has the most direct and non-linear relationship with the multiple a company can command. Companies with NRR below 90% trade at approximately 1.2x revenue, those with NRR of 100 to 110% trade at approximately 6x, and those above 120% command 8x or more (Windsor Drake, 2026). Crossing the 110% threshold matters disproportionately. A 30 to 50% higher multiple is typical for a business with 120% NRR versus a comparable one at 100% NRR, all else equal (Acquiry, 2026). A company that grows organically from within its existing customer base is, in the eyes of acquirers, a fundamentally different and more valuable asset.

To break down the psychology behind this, a loyal customer base will bring in more loyal customers as through word of mouth, they can bring in more ICP customers without costing anything to the company. This allows a multitude of companies and enterprises to hack the network effect. Additionally, it reinforces the credibility of the company's current metrics as churn will be low, life-time value of customers is expected to be high with growing switching costs, average contract or customer value is expected to grow. This brings credibility in the eyes of VCs and the "harder to win" customers. For example, large enterprise customers that are vulnerable to PR changes will be more skeptical of new software since integration is always costly and takes a long time to get everyone onboard and trained. For them, it is important that their vendor has long-term survivability, reinforces their external image and most importantly, is cost efficient after all the onboarding and software costs.

The Rule of 40

Meritech's regression analysis shows growth is 3.0x as correlated with multiple as FCF margin, meaning a 1% increase in growth has the same impact on multiple as a 3% increase in FCF margin (Meritech, 2026). Companies with similar Rule of 40 scores but growing faster trade at 8.7x, while slower-growing, higher-margin peers trade at just 3.3x (Meritech, 2026). Since in private markets, valuations are based on data and growth potential, it is much more decisive that public markets where the valuations are subject to Businesses that are EBITDA-positive or within 12 months of profitability command a 20 to 40% premium over comparable cash-burning peers (Acquiry, 2026).

The rule of 40 is a generalized benchmark for SaaS businesses that has developed over the SaaS boom. As the age of AI takes over and software becomes increasingly easy to build, it is still unclear if this metric is still applicable. Therefore, basing valuations on this metric is not ideal as the benchmarks are still yet to be accurately defined. Currently, the popular argument is that distribution is the MOAT and since the rule of 40 doesn't directly measure for that, it brings the question if this has become a vanity metric (Forbes, 2026).

The AI Premium and Vertical SaaS

According to reviewed articles, AI-native SaaS businesses are trading at 10x to 20x ARR compared to 4x to 7x for traditional SaaS (Kalungi, 2026). It is believed that it is reflecting genuine advantages in data network effects and unit economics. This is contrasting to public markets which are still compressing multiples for even profitable, AI-integrated businesses like HubSpot. This is signalling investors and founders that an AI positioning alone does not override growth quality and margin discipline (XMultiple, 2026). Vertical SaaS commands a 25 to 30% premium over horizontal SaaS at comparable performance levels (XMultiples, 2026).

The premiums are directly correlated to how much potential the ARR has the potential to grow. This is a very optimist approach to valuation certain companies and it makes it harder to compare different companies for those that have a foundational level of understanding SaaS valuation. Therefore, these optimist views make it harder for practitioners to cut through noise and signal in valuations. With the rise of social media, investors and firms are becoming increasingly more susceptible to group think. wIth the addition of premiums, it creates misinformed hype around certain companies which drives talent attraction, customer interest and potentially future valuations. This leads to a potential overvaluation and leads to the company not being able to execute to the expected value and a lack of "VC returns".

Conclusion

The metrics are telling a similar story to the SaaS overvaluations, but in a different era. Revenue multiples collapsed from their peaks as a repricing of capital efficiency against enterprise value. Growth still demonstrates the highest correlation with multiple when it is paired with strong retention and a credible path to profitability. The Rule of 40 has become the market's unified scoring system for that balance. NRR ties the valuation story together as a company with 120% or higher NRR is compounding from within and directly lifts both the Rule of 40 score and the achievable exit multiple. The AI premium and vertical specialization fit the same underlying logic as both represent moats that sustain retention and expansion rather than merely accelerating topline velocity.

With all that into consideration, a few metrics cannot tell the whole story on how the startup is doing. The metrics are based on historical data, but in the current day, the landscape of macroeconomics is changing daily with the advancement of AI. It is important for practitioners to create a story around the metrics and why they differ from the common benchmarks. The SaaS valuation is clearly becoming more of an art in the current era. Furthermore, as practitioners are able to gather more data, there will be another boom in technology that is also changing the landscape. So, in brief, it is important to constantly review how startups are being valued and build a framework that works for that specific company. There is never a perfect model, but the current results are similar to the past eras in different contexts. Therefore, the overall curve and returns are comparable and with the changes in the value of a dollar, the standard for good is increasing, making the gap between good and bad persist.

I believe current valuations are consistent with history. Afterall, most startups will fail, even in SaaS and AI.
