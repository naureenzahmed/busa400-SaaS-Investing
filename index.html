Overview on Venture Capital

A comprehensive overview of the venture capital and startup ecosystem — covering the different levels and types of VC firms, how they operate and build their fund thesis, the investment process from sourcing to close, and how startups can extract maximum value from their investors.

How Does a VC Firm Work?

What Does a Venture Capital Firm Do?

A venture capital (VC) firm is basically an investment manager. The main goal is to make a lot of money for the people and organizations that give it money. A VC firm is in the middle of a two-sided relationship. The Limited Partners (LPs) are the investors who give the money, and the General Partners (GPs) are the professionals who manage and invest that money in high-growth startups.

Pension funds, endowments, insurance companies, family offices, and high-net-worth individuals are all examples of large institutional investors that are LPs. There are a number of reasons why they put money into a VC fund. The main reason is money: historically, the best venture capital has made net returns of 20 to 30% per year, which is much higher than the long-term average for public equity markets. LPs invest in VC for more than just returns. They do it to get access to strategic co-investment opportunities, to diversify away from traditional asset classes like stocks and bonds, and to stay connected to new waves of innovation that could affect their business interests as a whole.

Cambridge Associates says that the US Venture Capital Index had a net return of 6.2% in 2024, bouncing back after two years of negative returns in 2022 and 2023. The picture is even more compelling over longer periods of time: the index made about 12% to 15% a year over the 25 years ending in 2023. The Cambridge Associates US VC Index made an average annual return of 32.4% from 1995 to 2020. The S&P 500, on the other hand, made an average annual return of 9.1% during the same time period. The problem is that these averages hide a lot of variation. The difference in net IRR between the top 25% and bottom 25% of VC funds is more than 30 percentage points, which is much bigger than in any other private equity strategy. In real life, picking the right fund is much more important than just putting money into the right asset class.

The high average returns on VC investments come from the very small chance of a big payoff. To get this average return without taking on a lot of risk, you need a very diverse portfolio. — National Bureau of Economic Research

Why Do Venture Capitalists Put Money Into New Businesses?

The reason venture capitalists invest in startups is because they have an uneven return profile. Most startups fail, but the ones that do succeed can make a lot more money than is possible in public markets.

Facebook is a classic example of this dynamic. In 2005, when Accel Partners put money into Facebook at a value of about $100 million, the site was a small college social network with no proven business model. When Facebook went public in 2012, it was worth about $100 billion. Accel's investment had grown by about 1,000 times its original value. On the other hand, an investor who bought Facebook shares on the public market at the IPO price would have made about three times their money over the next ten years. This is a good return, but it's not even close to the venture-level upside.

This is the main reason why venture capital is an asset class. VCs are okay with illiquidity, uncertainty, and high failure rates because they want to be able to invest in the next big company as soon as possible, when prices are lowest and ownership stakes are highest.

Most startups (90%) fail in the end. But one big investment can pay off many times over for a whole fund. The VC model is based on this power-law dynamic, which is why portfolio construction and diversification are so important.
Harvard Business School / Failory Research, 2024

Why Put Money Into More Than One Startup?

Startups lose money on cash flow; they need outside funding all the time to stay alive. A single bad outcome, like a technical failure, a market rejection, or a competitive disruption, can completely destroy an investment. Because of this, VCs make sure their portfolios are diverse. Each fund usually has between 10 and 30 companies, which helps spread risk across many positions.

This diversification is necessary because of the statistical reality of venture returns. Data on 21,640 venture financings from 2004 to 2013 shows that 64.8% of the investments returned between zero and one times the money put in, which means they were losses. Another 25.3% gave back between one and five times the original investment. Only 5.9% came back 5 to 10 times, and only 0.4% came back more than 50 times. At the median, a $100 million portfolio that invested in 10 companies would make about $206 million, or a 10% internal rate of return. Also, if you take out the investments that made more than five times the money you put in, that same portfolio has a negative return. This is the most important thing to know about venture investing: a small number of outliers support the whole fund.

According to research from Harvard Business School, about 75% of startups that get money from investors never give it back. Investors lose all of their money in 30 to 40% of cases. This is how the system works: the few breakout companies make up for the many that never reached their full potential.

How Is a VC Fund Structured?

The way a venture capital fund works follows a clear cycle that usually lasts ten years, but it can be extended by one year if needed. To understand how VCs make decisions, why they act the way they do, and what makes them interested in a particular startup, you need to know this cycle.

Stage 1
Raising Money

The GP starts the process by getting money from LPs. Banks, corporations, endowments, sovereign wealth funds, and wealthy people are all sources of capital. Fundraising is a constant process. When one fund's investment period ends, the GP starts to raise the next one. This puts constant pressure on LPs to show that they are doing well and keep good relationships with LPs.

Stage 2
Investment and Evaluation

The GP starts looking at startup opportunities and putting money to work after the fund has been raised. The commitment period is the first five years of the fund's life. This is usually the only time when new investments are made. The due diligence section below goes into great detail about how to evaluate a startup.

Stage 3
Keeping an Eye on Things

After they make an investment, the VC is in charge of running the portfolio company and making sure it grows profitably. This includes being on the board, giving strategic advice, making introductions to potential customers and partners, and helping to hire senior staff. Putting money into a startup is just the first step; VCs make more money by helping the founder grow their business.

Stage 4
Exit

An exit event creates liquidity. This event usually happens in one of three ways: an initial public offering (IPO), a merger or acquisition (M&A), or, in the worst case, a failure and wind-down. The Dow Jones VentureSource database tracks data that shows that about 12% of early-stage investors' portfolio companies go public (IPO) and about 19% go through a merger or acquisition (M&A). About 38% of companies that reach exit fail. Late-stage investors, who usually put money into older companies, see higher rates of mergers and acquisitions (50%) and initial public offerings (60%). There were a lot fewer exits in North America and Europe between 2014 and 2016, because companies stayed private longer, and this trend has continued into the current decade.

Stage 5
Distribution

After exits happen, capital and investment proceeds are sent back to both LPs and GPs according to a waterfall structure that was agreed upon ahead of time. This distribution means that the fund's life cycle is over.

How Long Does a Typical Venture Fund Last?

Most Limited Partnership Agreements (LPAs) let a standard venture fund last for ten years, but they can be extended for up to three one-year periods, bringing the total to thirteen years. The GP makes new investments during the first five years, which is called the commitment or investment period. After the fifth year, the fund goes into its harvesting phase. This means that no new companies are added to the portfolio, but the GP keeps investing in existing portfolio companies and works hard to get them to leave.

A new fund is usually raised between the third and fifth years of the current fund, before the first fund's investment period ends. Because of this overlap, a GP often has to manage an existing portfolio while raising and deploying a new fund at the same time. This takes a lot of bandwidth and organizational structure.

The fund's life can be divided into three distinct phases:

  • Formation (Year 0): Getting money, setting up the legal structure, and setting up the taxes. The GP sets up the fund entity, works out the terms with the LP, and does all the initial planning.
  • Investment (Years 0 to 5): Finding deals, doing due diligence, putting money into new investments, and making early follow-on investments. The investment team is busiest during this phase of operations.
  • Harvesting (Years 5 to 10+): Getting the portfolio ready for exit, helping portfolio companies grow, making follow-on investments in the best performers, and finally giving LPs their money back.

How Do VCs Make Money?

There are two ways that GPs get paid: a management fee and carried interest, which is often called "carry."

The management fee is usually 2% of the committed capital per year. This fee covers the fund's operating costs, such as salaries, rent, travel, and legal fees. This makes $2 million a year, or about $20 million over the ten-year life of a $100 million fund. This fee is not a profit; it's part of the operating budget.

Carried interest is how GPs make real money. Carry is usually 20% of the fund's profits over a hurdle rate, which is usually around 8%. This is also known as a preferred return. The mechanics work like this, using a simple example of a top-decile fund:

  • The LPs give the fund $100 million.
  • After paying $20 million in fees and costs, $80 million is put into portfolio companies.
  • Of those investments, $20 million is written off as losses, so there is still $60 million in active holdings.
  • The remaining portfolio grows to a value of $240 million, which is three times the amount of money that was invested.
  • After paying back the $80 million in invested capital, the $20 million in fees, and any preferred return obligations, there is still $120 million left.
  • Out of that $120 million, LPs get 80% ($96 million) and the GP gets 20% ($24 million) as carried interest.

This structure makes sure that GPs and LPs have the same goals: GPs only get paid well when their investments make more money than the hurdle rate. A fund that only returns capital doesn't make the GP any money other than the management fee.

How to Source a Deal

What Does Deal Sourcing Mean for a VC Firm?

When it comes to venture capital, not all companies are the same. Deal flow, or the number, quality, and timing of investment opportunities that a firm can get, is a big part of what makes a top-decile fund different from a median fund. Deal sourcing is the first step in the whole venture capital process. Without it, even the most thorough due diligence process has nothing to work with. That's why venture capital (VC) firms try to reach as many people as possible to find out if they are making the best investments in the market.

The best people in the industry are aware of this dynamic. Top venture capital firms don't just sit around and wait for founders to come to them. They create proactive, multi-channel sourcing engines that look for the best startups as soon as possible, sometimes even before those startups have any public profile.

How Does the Competition Look for Finding Deals?

Data from the National Venture Capital Association and PitchBook show that US VC managers made more than 14,600 deals and spent more than $213 billion in 2024 alone. But even with this many deals, the basic truth about venture investing hasn't changed: most of the returns come from a very small number of companies.

Because returns are so concentrated, getting the right deals is very important. The people who started those companies with a lot of potential also know how much power they have. The best founders often get more than one term sheet at once, and the price is not the only thing that affects their decision about which company to work with. A founder's choice of investor depends on the brand, network, operational support, and speed of execution. This is why deal sourcing is a two-way competition: VCs not only want to find the best startups, but they also want to convince those startups to take their money instead of other options.

The ecosystem has grown too big, spread out too much, and moved too quickly for one person or a small team to cover it all through personal relationships alone. Because of this, top companies have spent a lot of money on building systematic, scalable sourcing infrastructure that works with traditional relationship-driven approaches and adds new tools, programs, and data capabilities.

Scout Programs

A VC firm works with a distributed network of operators, founders, academics, and investors in innovation hotspots around the world as part of a scout program. These scouts are paid, usually with a small share of the carry in deals they find, to find and recommend high-potential founders to the firm before those founders start a formal fundraising process.

The scout program at Sequoia Capital is one of the most famous in the business. It has been said that it has brought in deals that would never have made it to the firm's funnel through normal channels. The program works because scouts have real, trustworthy connections in their own communities that other VC firms can't make from far away. Also, scouts bring different points of view that can help find opportunities in communities that aren't well-represented and with founders who don't fit the mold. Scout programs also add an extra layer of evaluation because scouts do an informal pre-screening before sending companies to you. This makes the leads you get at the top of the funnel better.

Funds for Students

Student funds are a complementary strategy that focuses on the university campus, which is the first step in starting a business. These vehicles are driven by trained college students and use pre-seed capital to help promising student entrepreneurs who are still in school start businesses. In North America, Dorm Room Fund and Front Row Ventures are two well-known examples of this model. Student fund programs also help train the next generation of venture capitalists by providing a steady stream of trained analysts and associates who know how the firm invests. They do due diligence on very early-stage opportunities at a low cost because student fund teams do the first evaluation work before a deal gets to the sponsoring firm.

Venture Studios

Venture studios, also known as startup studios or company builders, build, launch, and run several startups at the same time from within the company itself. The studio has everything you need to run multiple new businesses at once, including product teams, designers, legal support, marketing tools, and access to a network of experts.

The model has a number of strong benefits for VC firms that run or work with venture studios. The startup process is less risky because experienced operators are involved from the beginning and use what they learned from building other companies. When resources are shared, it lowers the burn rates across the portfolio by creating economies of scale. Before making any formal investment decision, it has a very clear view of the team's skills, the product's progress, and the business model's viability. Companies that are built in a studio tend to have a higher survival rate than companies that are started on their own, because the model gives them structural support.

Feeder Funds

Feeder funds let established VC firms reach more people by investing in new fund managers who are putting money into markets, geographies, or verticals by backing smaller, more specialized funds. This gives them access to their deal flow, learnings, and portfolio companies.

The Discovery Fund from Inovia Capital is a well-known example of this model in action. It focuses on investing in new managers to make the Canadian tech funding ecosystem stronger. The fund sets up a win-win situation: new managers get capital and institutional credibility, and Inovia gets to see a wider range of companies and markets than it could through direct investment alone. The feeder fund model also helps reduce risk by giving the investor exposure to a variety of managers and strategies instead of putting all their eggs in one basket.

Service Offerings

Some of the biggest VC firms in the world have built whole platforms of services to draw in founders and keep in touch with the startup ecosystem. Andreessen Horowitz (a16z) helps its portfolio companies run their businesses by giving them access to curated networks and events, operational support, advisory services through a library of company-building content, platform services, and technical expertise. These services are a way for the company to find deals because they give founders a reason to work with them even before they need money.

Service platforms get founders interested in the company by showing that it can be more than just a source of money. A company that is known for really helping founders will attract better founders over time, creating a cycle of deal quality improvement that keeps going.

EIRs and Referral Networks

Entrepreneurs in Residence (EIRs) are experienced business owners or former founders who work for a company for a set amount of time. During that time, they may give feedback on how existing portfolio companies are doing, look into new investment opportunities, or work on starting a new company that the firm will invest in. The best thing about the EIR model for finding deals is that when an EIR starts a new business, the founding team is already vetted. The firm already knows the person's skills, work ethic, and judgment in a way that cold inbound deal flow can't.

Zapflow's research shows that referral networks always offer the best investment opportunities for VC firms. This is because the person who refers the opportunity has already done some informal screening and is putting their own reputation on the line by making the introduction. Leads from referral networks are usually pre-vetted, come with useful information, and turn into investments at much higher rates than cold inbounds.

Conferences and Events for Networking

Conferences bring together investors, founders, service providers, and ecosystem players in settings that encourage casual conversation and exploration. Being at important events on a regular basis builds the company's reputation and brand in the ecosystem, showing that they are serious about a certain area or sector. It makes connections that often pay off months or years later, when the founder decides to raise money. One of the less well-known parts of a strong sourcing strategy is how consistent event presence over time can have a compounding effect. "In real life" is the only thing that sets them apart, especially now that AI and automation are common. It also touches on the basics of human nature, like the need for support and connection. When founders are having a hard time and need help, they can call the partner who is there to help them.

Steps in the Investment Process

Investing in startups is a structured process that starts with finding an opportunity and ends with becoming a trusted partner in the founder's journey. Every step helps you feel more sure of yourself and gives you an edge over your competitors.

Step 1
Finding the Deal

The best venture capitalists create multi-channel sourcing engines that include referral networks, scout programs, student funds, conferences, and data-driven inbound platforms. The goal is to spot the best opportunities early, before competition drives up prices and makes it harder to own.

Step 2
Thesis Fit

Before spending any time with a company, a VC must ask themselves one question: does this company fit within our fund's investment mandate? Thesis alignment includes stage, sector, geography, and check size. If a deal is objectively strong but not in the fund's area of expertise, it should be passed on. This is because investments that are not aligned with the fund's goals can weaken its expertise and stretch its resources because they can't invest because of their LPAC.

Step 3
Due Diligence

Due diligence is focused, not all-encompassing. The most important factors are the team (95% of VCs say this is the most important), the market (it should be big, growing, and not well served), the competition (it should be easy to tell the difference), the product (it should show real value creation), the business model (it should have sound unit economics and a scalable go-to-market), and the risks (what needs to go right and what could go wrong). The process is based on chance, not certainty. The goal is to come up with a strong investment thesis, not to get rid of all doubt. It is also to see if the team can handle the uncertainties and do what they need to do.

Step 4
The Investment Committee

There are two ways to approve deals: the conviction model, where one partner can champion and close an investment, and the consensus model, where most or all partners must agree. In either case, the partner who is sponsoring the project must be able to explain a clear investment thesis and a strong case for getting a return. The best IC presentations show that the hardest questions have already been asked and that there are no objections.

Step 5
Getting the Founder

The best founders pick their investors as carefully as investors pick their founders. A VC wins allocation by showing that they can offer more than just money, like making useful introductions to people in their network, sharing their knowledge of the industry, giving operational advice, and having a history of helping founders through tough times. Founders want more than just money; they want a partner.

Step 6
Winning Allocation

In competitive rounds, you get allocation based on how fast you are, how well you get along with others, and how much value you think you add. Getting to the term sheet first, offering better terms than other bidders, or giving a strategic asset that no one else has, like introducing a key customer or putting a board member with direct category expertise on the board, can make or break a fund's ability to get its target ownership.

Step 7
Making Your Portfolio Worth More

Active board participation, help with hiring the right people, introductions to customers and investors, and honest advice on how to run the business all add to the value of an investment. The VC's return depends on how well the founder does, so they have a duty to help after the investment both as a fiduciary and as a matter of reputation.

These seven steps don't make a linear list; instead, they show how rigor, judgment, and trust build on each other.

How Startups Can Extract Value from VC Firms

There is a well-known hole in the middle of venture capital. The Forward More Than Money report from 2021 says that 92% of VC firms say they are value-add investors. Only 61% of founders agree, and 65% say their investors tried but failed. It's clear what this means: giving value beyond money isn't automatic. It needs to be planned, structured, and really focused on the founder.

1. Introductions to Customers and Networks

Access to networks is the type of VC support that people value the most. A study found that 33% of founders say that an investor's connections are a key factor in the success of a business (Forward More Than Money, 2021). This includes introductions to potential customers, strategic partners, and ways to get the word out about your business. A VC who starts a meaningful business relationship with a startup can help it get to market months faster. Notably, 63% of US-based founders say that customer introductions are one of the top three things they look for when choosing a VC.

2. Help with Fundraising

Help getting more money in the future is the second most valuable thing. Founders see VCs as natural ways to get to the next round. In a competitive fundraising environment, a VC's warm introductions to co-investors, its signal of continued confidence through pro-rata participation, and its coaching on investor narrative and data room preparation can make all the difference. Jolyon Martin from PetMedix has said that this is the best thing an investor can do to add value.

3. Finding and Hiring Talented People

For most early-stage companies, hiring is the hardest part of running their business. Forbes (2025) reports on research that shows that portfolio companies that get active talent support from their VCs cut the time it takes to hire by 40% and raise the acceptance rates of executives by 60%. VCs can use their networks to find candidates, cut down on the need for expensive recruiters, and help founders tell equity stories that draw in top talent.

4. Advice on Strategy and Operations

VC investors can spot patterns across many portfolios that founders, who only work for one company, can't. This includes guides on how to set prices, how to sell, how to prioritize product development, and how to deal with crises. Research from ScienceDirect shows that VC-backed companies with active investor monitoring see much higher productivity growth in the years after their first round of investment. This is because of coaching and value-adding services, not just the money itself.

5. Help with the Board and Governance

When board members are involved, investors can hold people accountable in a structured way. Founders get a governance structure that grows with the company from VCs who come prepared, question assumptions in a helpful way, and help set realistic operating goals. The ScienceDirect literature on VC monitoring further establishes that closer VC-CEO interaction is most valuable at the earliest stages, when uncertainty is highest and the need for external perspective is greatest.

6. Community of Portfolio and Peer Learning

A good VC firm adds value to the whole portfolio, not just the individual companies. More and more people are realizing that connecting founders who are facing similar growth challenges, helping portfolio companies share knowledge, and putting on events that encourage peer learning are all important ways to stand out.

Companies that close the gap between what they promise and what founders actually get are the ones that plan ahead for post-investment support instead of leaving it up to chance.

Finding the Right Investment

How VC firms build and apply their investment thesis, evaluate potential deals, construct portfolios across fund vintages, and navigate portfolio adjustments when market conditions shift.

How to Match a Firm's Investment Thesis and Portfolio to Potential Deals

A venture capital firm's investment thesis is basically the backbone of how it makes decisions. It deeply describes out what the firm believes about where the world is heading, which markets are worth betting on, and what kinds of companies the partners are genuinely well-positioned to help. Matching that thesis to incoming deals works as both a filtering tool and a reflection of what the firm is actually good at and focuses on. Without this kind of alignment, firms end up diluting their value-add and leaving LPs confused about what they actually believe in.

Fred Wilson of Union Square Ventures has written a lot about the difference between thematic and thesis-driven investing. Thematic investing picks up on broad macro trends like mobility, decentralization, or AI, and spreads bets across companies that ride those waves. Thesis-driven investing is more specific. It makes a concrete, testable claim about how a market will evolve, then looks for companies that fit that picture. USV's well-known thesis around large networks of engaged users, differentiated through user experience and defensible through network effects, guided investments in Twitter, Etsy, and Coinbase. Every deal they looked at was held against that standard first. Julius Bachmann points out in Hackernoon that this kind of specificity is what lets early-stage firms move fast and with real conviction. When you know what you are looking for, you recognize it quickly, because it becomes pattern matching within the winner of that industry and stage.

Matching a deal to a thesis is not just running through a checklist. Investors at firms like Andreessen Horowitz and Sequoia actively map their existing portfolio before sourcing new deals. To expose themselves to as much diversification as possible, they ask things like: where are we underexposed? What stage, geography, or sector are we missing? Little Engine Ventures calls this a portfolio gap analysis, which involves reviewing current positions to figure out where a new investment would add optionality or balance out existing concentration. If a fund is already heavily weighted toward B2B SaaS, piling into another deal in the same space might not just be redundant. It could create conflicts of interest or reduce the portfolio's ability to generate outsized outcomes.

The thesis also sends a signal to founders. Sam Altman has noted that the best founders in any category tend to seek out investors who genuinely understand their market. A firm without a clear thesis often ends up seeing the deals that nobody else wanted. Thesis clarity works as both an evaluation filter and a sourcing engine. It pulls in the exact founders the firm is best set up to support.

Beyond thesis fit, portfolio matching means thinking carefully about pro-rata rights, signaling risk, and stage concentration. If a firm has already written big Series A checks across a sector, leading another seed deal in that same space might read to the market as a sign the Series A position is struggling. According to Contrary Research, portfolio construction discipline requires intentional sequencing. It is about knowing not just which deals to do, but when and in what proportion. Matching thesis to deals is really an act of active portfolio management, not just picking individual companies.

How VC Firms Pick Investments and the Factors of Consideration

VC investment involve fast pattern-matching against past experience, genuine conviction about teams, and a real structural read on market opportunity. Underneath what looks like pure gut feel, most high-performing firms are actually applying pretty consistent frameworks. Understanding how those frameworks work is useful for anyone who operates in or around the venture world.

Market Size

Market size is usually the first thing investors look at. A16z's foundational piece on startup metrics (2015) emphasized that investors need to assess total addressable market with some healthy skepticism. The market has to be big enough to support a venture-scale return. A $1B market can produce a solid business, but it rarely produces a fund-returning company. Mike Vernal of Sequoia has written about the market curve, pointing out that the best companies often create their own markets rather than just capturing existing ones. That means evaluating not just where the TAM is today, but where it plausibly goes as the company itself grows.

Team Evaluation

Team evaluation is the biggest factor at early stages. First Round Capital's diligence process stresses that product intuition, domain expertise, and founder-market fit are often more predictive than the business model itself. Investors want founders who have spent real time close to the problem, not just intellectually but in practice. The core question is whether they have a genuinely differentiated point of view and the grit to survive when early assumptions turn out to be wrong. The team needs to be able to figure it out when all goes wrong and all the odds are stacked against them.

Product and Technology Differentiation

Product and technology differentiation comes right after. GoingVC's due diligence framework recommends checking whether the core tech is proprietary, whether the product is solving a real pain or just a nice-to-have, and whether the unit economics hold up at scale.

Competitive Dynamics and Timing

Competitive dynamics and timing also matter a lot. Arteen Arabshahi's marketplace calculator framework shows that for marketplace businesses, whether the unit economics ever close depends a lot on the structural balance between supply and demand, and which side the company actually controls. A company entering a market a year too early burns cash building supply-side infrastructure nobody is ready to use. A year too late and they cannot displace an incumbent with strong network effects. The EC Early Stage diligence framework explicitly scores timing as a variable for this reason.

Valuation

Even a great company is a bad investment if the price is wrong. A16z's 16 startup metrics framework includes burn multiple, revenue growth rate, and net dollar retention as core diligence inputs. A company growing at 3x per year but burning 10x revenue to get there is not building durable value. It is basically borrowing growth against future dilution. Price discipline is what keeps disciplined venture from sliding into speculative capital deployment.

Considerations for Portfolio Construction Fund Over Fund

Portfolio construction in venture capital is genuinely complex, and it gets more complex across fund vintages. Each fund has to balance power-law return dynamics, stage concentration, sector diversification, reserve management, and LP expectations while staying consistent with the firm's investment thesis. Done poorly, a firm can produce strong individual deals but mediocre fund-level returns. Done well, portfolio construction sets up the conditions for at least one company to return the entire fund.

The foundational insight here comes from power law math. Jerry Neumann's analysis at Reaction Wheel shows clearly that VC returns are not normally distributed. The top one or two investments in a portfolio typically account for a wildly disproportionate share of total returns. This means concentration and portfolio size pull against each other. A portfolio of 40 seed investments has better statistical coverage than one of 10, but if you write smaller checks to get that breadth, you may not own enough to matter when a winner emerges. Fred Wilson has noted that USV typically targets 20 to 25 companies per fund, large enough to expect a few breakouts, small enough to maintain meaningful ownership and stay engaged at the board level.

Reserve ratios are probably the most consequential operational decision in portfolio construction. The ICONIQ SaaS Glossary notes that institutional funds typically reserve 50 to 60 percent of fund capital for follow-on investments. The companies most deserving of follow-on capital are the ones already in your portfolio performing well. Not reserving enough forces you to either dilute at later rounds or pass on pro-rata rights, both of which erode your position in the companies that matter most.

Fund-over-fund construction also means learning from vintage-level mistakes. If Fund I was overweighted in consumer, and consumer returned poorly, Fund II might shift toward enterprise SaaS — as a reactive bet, but also as a considered response to where the partners actually have an informational edge. Mostly Metrics makes the point that SaaS businesses specifically benefit from investors who can benchmark metrics against cohorts of similar-stage companies. Furthermore, firms that have deployed two or more funds into SaaS accumulate that benchmarking advantage.

Loss ratios need to be anticipated and built into the construction logic. Wilson from AVC writes that in early-stage VC, roughly 40 to 50 percent of investments will result in a loss of capital, 20 to 30 percent will return capital or a small multiple, and 20 to 30 percent will generate meaningful returns. The power law math means this distribution is not a failure. It is the expected outcome of a diversified early-stage portfolio. A firm that engineers its portfolio specifically to minimize loss ratios is probably over-indexing on safety and underweighting the big bets that return funds.

Across funds, the path from Fund I to Fund III typically involves increasing specialization, tighter ownership targets, and more disciplined reserve policies. Battery Ventures' State of the OpenCloud highlights that cloud software portfolios built with discipline around ARR growth benchmarks and net retention thresholds tend to produce more consistent fund-level returns than generalist approaches. Each fund vintage is a proof point, not just to LPs, but to the partners themselves, about what their actual edge is.

How Portfolio Adjustments Are Made in Changing Market Conditions

Market dislocations like rising interest rates, contracting public multiples, tightening LP capital, or sector-specific corrections force GPs to rethink their portfolio strategy in real time. How a firm manages its portfolio during downturns is honestly just as consequential as how it picks investments during good times. The calls around down rounds, reserve deployment, triage, and follow-on pacing can determine whether a fund's best companies actually survive long enough to become defining outcomes.

When a company raises at a valuation below its prior round, existing investors face dilution, anti-dilution provisions kick in, and the signal to the market is not great. The Equals SaaS Metrics Guide notes that in a down round environment, the key metric to watch is burn multiple, which tracks how many dollars of capital are burned to generate each new dollar of net new ARR. A company with a burn multiple above 2x in a down market is unlikely to attract outside capital without meaningfully restructuring its operations. Investors have to make a call: does this company have a real path to profitability with a bridge, or is putting more money in just delaying the inevitable?

Reserve deployment becomes a lot more strategic during market corrections. Firms that over-deployed reserves into follow-ons during the 2021 bull market found themselves without dry powder when portfolio companies needed bridge financing in 2022 and 2023. The lesson from that period, covered well by Axios Pro Rata, is that reserve pacing needs to be countercyclical. You want to be holding reserves for when companies need capital most, which is exactly when external capital is hardest to raise. A common rule of thumb is to reserve enough for at least two additional rounds for each core portfolio position.

Cutting out, meaning declining to participate in a follow-on, is one of the harder decisions a VC faces. Not following on sends a signal to co-investors and to the market. However, continuing to invest in underperforming companies just to avoid sending that signal is what gets called zombie financing, and it eats fund returns while consuming GP time. GoingVC's due diligence framework suggests developing explicit decision criteria before you even make an investment. Under what conditions will you not follow on? Forcing that conversation during initial underwriting rather than in the middle of a tense board meeting produces cleaner decisions later.

When a sector re-prices, the same company that looked expensive at Series B can suddenly look quite reasonable. SaaS multiples compressing from 15x ARR to 6x ARR is a good example. The Pmarchive writing from Marc Andreessen makes the point that market timing is less predictable than market quality, and that the best time to invest in a great company is often when others are pulling back. Firms with dry powder during contractions should generally be leaning in.

On the operational side, portfolio adjustments during downturns require a triage mindset. Wilson suggests bucketing portfolio companies into three groups: those that will survive regardless of support, those that need help and capital to have a real shot, and those that are unlikely to return capital no matter what the firm does. GP time is the scarcest resource in the building. Allocating it heavily toward the middle bucket, the companies that are actually salvageable, while accepting the write-offs in the third group, is the most value-maximizing approach. Downturns are painful, but they do clarify which companies were built on real foundations and which were just products of cheap capital.

Metrics  /  Overview

SaaS Evaluation Metrics

The core metrics used to evaluate venture-backed SaaS companies — from unit economics and cohort retention to efficiency ratios and fund-level performance — and how firms weight them based on stage, thesis, and LP commitments.

What Are the Main Components and Metrics Looked at in Venture-Backed SaaS Companies?

From the moment a SaaS company acquires its first customer, it is burning more cash than it takes in. The initial sale rarely covers the cost of acquiring that customer. Instead, the company recoups that upfront investment over time through recurring subscription fees. This means earnings look terrible early on even when the business is actually healthy.

CAC — Customer Acquisition Cost

The most foundational metric in this picture is Customer Acquisition Cost, or CAC. In a SaaS context, CAC is how much it costs in total sales and marketing spend to bring in one new subscriber. There are two versions worth tracking. One looks only at program costs like ad spend and events. The other is fully-loaded and includes the salaries and overhead of the entire sales and marketing team. The fully-loaded CAC is the more conservative and honest number, and it tends to look a lot worse in the early days of a SaaS business when the team is expensive, but the customer base is still small.

LTV — Lifetime Value

On the other side of the equation is Lifetime Value, or LTV. In a SaaS business, LTV estimates how much gross profit a customer will generate over the entire duration of their subscription. LTV should be based on gross margin, because the actual cost to serve a SaaS customer including hosting, support, and infrastructure meaningfully reduces what you are actually keeping from each subscription dollar. The rule of thumb that most investors use is that LTV should be at least three times CAC for the business to be structurally viable. If you are spending more acquiring customers than you will ever make back from them on a gross margin basis, the subscription model is not working regardless of how fast revenue is growing.

ARR — Annual Recurring Revenue

Annual Recurring Revenue is the core revenue metric for SaaS. It converts the value of all active subscriptions into an annualized run rate, giving a cleaner picture of the revenue base than booking or billings numbers. The more useful view breaks ARR into its moving parts: Gross New ARR (total) and Expansion ARR (from existing customers who upgraded or added seats), Contraction ARR (downgrades), and Churned ARR (cancellations). Looking at each component separately matters because a company can report strong Net ARR while quietly losing its best customers, masked by aggressive new logo acquisition. That is a very different and much more dangerous business than one where the ARR growth is coming from a healthy mix of new customers and expansion.

Retention — GDR and NDR

Retention is where the SaaS model either proves itself or falls apart. There are two main retention metrics investors track: GDR and NDR. Gross Dollar Retention measures how much of last period's revenue was kept from existing customers after accounting for churn and contraction but before any expansion. It is a ceiling metric — no matter how good your expansion motion is, you cannot have NDR above your GDR. Net Dollar Retention adds expansion revenue back in on top of GDR. NDR above 100 percent is one of the most important signals in SaaS investing, because it means the existing customer base is growing on its own. A SaaS business with 120 percent NDR is structurally compounding. Investors typically want to see 110 to 120 percent NDR as a baseline for growth-stage SaaS companies, and anything above 120 percent is considered exceptional.

Burn Multiple and Magic Number

The Burn Multiple divides net cash burn by net new ARR over the same period. It answers the question every investor is now asking post-2022: for every dollar of new recurring revenue added, how many dollars of cash did it cost to get there? A burn multiple under 1 means the company is generating more new ARR than it is burning, which is outstanding. Above 2 starts to raise questions about whether the go-to-market is working efficiently. The Magic Number is a related metric that looks specifically at sales and marketing ROI, measuring how much net new ARR was generated for each dollar of S&M spend in the prior period. Both metrics are useful lenses on the same underlying question: is this company growing in a way that will eventually produce cash, or is it just buying revenue with investor capital?

Payback Period

Payback period tells you how many months of subscription gross margin it takes to cover the CAC for a new customer. If payback is 24 months, you need two years of a customer staying subscribed and paying before you break even on acquiring them, before any contribution to overhead or profit. For companies selling to SMBs, where churn is naturally higher, a 24-month payback is risky. For enterprise SaaS with multi-year contracts and high switching costs, it is more manageable. Most growth-stage SaaS investors consider 12 months a healthy payback range.

What Are the Different Types of SaaS Metrics and How Do They Differ by Level?

One of the most useful ways to organize SaaS metrics is to recognize that they operate at different levels and answer different questions.

Level 1
Unit Economics

At the unit economics level, the question is simple: is each individual customer relationship profitable, and by how much? This is where ARPA (Average Revenue Per Account), LTV, CAC, and payback period all live. In a SaaS business, every new customer starts as a money-losing relationship because the cost to acquire them typically exceeds their first month or even first year of subscription revenue. A SaaS company with 60 percent gross margins is in a very different unit economics position than one running at 80 percent, even if their ARR looks the same on the surface.

Level 2
Cohort Analysis

At the cohort level, you are zooming out from individual customers to groups of customers acquired during the same period, usually a month or quarter. This is where retention analysis really matters and where the difference between cohorted and uncohorted metrics is critical to understand. Uncohorted retention looks at aggregate churn across the whole customer base and is easy to misread. A SaaS company that is growing fast by adding lots of new customers can look like it has low churn on an uncohorted basis, even if older cohorts are leaking badly. The new customer volume masks the problem. Cohorted retention tracks what percentage of each acquisition cohort is still subscribed at specified periods — this gives a much more honest view of whether the product is actually sticky.

Level 3
Business Efficiency

At the business efficiency level, metrics like Burn Multiple, Magic Number, and ARR Multiple answer a higher-level question: is the whole go-to-market machine working? Burn Multiple is the most widely used efficiency signal — a catch-all metric that flags whether something is off without necessarily isolating what. A high burn multiple in a SaaS business could mean the sales team is spending too much on customer acquisition, it could mean gross margins are too low, or it could mean the company is in a full-build phase where heavy R&D spend is needed to compete and the burn will normalize once the product is complete. It needs to be paired with CAC, gross margin, and cohort retention to figure out which situation you are actually dealing with.

Level 4
Fund-Level Performance

At the fund level, the relevant numbers are IRR (Internal Rate of Return), TVPI (Total Value to Paid-In Capital), DPI (Distributions to Paid-In Capital), and RVPI (Residual Value to Paid-In). IRR is the annualized return on capital accounting for the timing of cash flows. TVPI is the total current value of the fund divided by total capital called, combining both what has been returned and what is still held. DPI specifically measures realized returns, meaning actual cash back to LPs, and is the metric that grows in importance as a fund matures. In early-stage SaaS-focused funds, DPI tends to sit near zero for the first several years, because SaaS exits take time to materialize. By year eight or nine, DPI is what LPs are watching most closely.

How Do Firms Think About Metrics Depending on Their Thesis and LP Commitments?

The metrics a firm emphasizes are directly shaped by their investment thesis, the stage they focus on, and the reporting commitments they have made to their LPs. A seed fund writing small checks into pre-product SaaS companies is working with almost no quantitative signal. A growth fund leading Series B rounds into companies with multiple millions in ARR is operating in a completely different information environment.

At the pre-seed and seed stage, the traditional SaaS metrics are often not there yet or are too noisy to be meaningful. A company with 15 customers does not have statistically reliable CAC or LTV numbers. What early-stage SaaS investors actually look at is more qualitative and directional. Are early customers actually using the product, measured by engagement metrics like DAU or session frequency? Are the handful of cohorts available showing any sign of flattening retention, even if the numbers are small? Is there any evidence of organic expansion within early accounts? These are weak signals, but they are the only signals available, and experienced seed investors have learned to read them carefully.

For growth-stage funds with a B2B SaaS thesis, the metrics framework becomes much more rigorous and the benchmarks get tighter fast. NDR moves from a nice-to-have signal to a dealbreaker. A growth fund evaluating a Series B SaaS opportunity will typically want to see NDR above 110 percent before they get comfortable with the retention story. They will look hard at cohorted retention at months 12 and 24, checking whether the retention curve is actually flattening or just decelerating its decline. Gross margin matters a lot at this stage too. SaaS companies should be targeting 75 to 80 percent gross margins at scale, and anything significantly below that raises questions about whether the business is genuinely software or whether it has a people-heavy delivery model hiding underneath the SaaS label. ARR growth rate expectations are also demanding — for a competitive Series A or B round in SaaS, 2x to 3x year-over-year ARR growth is generally the baseline, not the stretch.

The investment thesis also directly shapes how firms weigh efficiency versus growth metrics. During the 2020 to 2022 period, when capital was cheap and exit multiples were high, many SaaS-focused funds were willing to overlook burn multiples above 3 as long as ARR growth was strong. Post-2022, when public SaaS multiples compressed dramatically and the fundraising environment tightened, that logic broke. LP sentiment shifted quickly toward capital efficiency. Funds revised their portfolio review criteria to weight burn multiple, payback period, and gross margin quality much more heavily.

LP clauses add a concrete layer of structure to all of this. Many Limited Partnership Agreements include specific reporting requirements tied to portfolio company metrics. Growth funds that have committed their LPs to quarterly reporting on NDR, ARR growth, and gross margin have a direct incentive to make sure their portfolio companies actually track and report those numbers in a consistent and auditable way. Some institutional LPs, particularly university endowments or fund-of-funds with their own reporting obligations, require both company-level metrics and fund-level performance reporting across IRR, TVPI, and DPI every quarter. This creates a real transmission belt from LP requirements to how GPs manage their portfolio.

There is also a real tension between portfolio-level SaaS metrics and fund-level performance metrics that does not get discussed enough. A fund with a thesis around capital-efficient B2B SaaS might have portfolio companies that look great on burn multiple and NDR but have not yet reached exit-scale. In that scenario, fund-level DPI stays near zero even as the underlying companies compound well. This is structurally normal for SaaS, where exit timelines tend to be longer than in consumer or marketplace businesses, but it creates friction with LPs who care about actual cash distributions. Funds that back longer-hold SaaS businesses need a clear and proactive narrative for their LPs about the exit pathway and timeline. Thesis clarity is not just about sourcing and due diligence — in the LP relationship, it is what makes the reporting conversation make sense.
