Overview of the VC & Startup Space

A comprehensive overview of the venture capital and startup ecosystem — covering the different levels and types of VC firms, how they operate and build their fund thesis, the investment process from sourcing to close, and how startups can extract maximum value from their investors.

How Does a VC Firm Work?

What is a Venture Capital Firm?

At its core, a venture capital (VC) firm is an investment manager. Its primary objective is to generate strong financial returns for the individuals and institutions that entrust it with capital. A VC firm sits at the center of a two-sided relationship: on one side are the Limited Partners (LPs), the investors who provide the capital, and on the other side are the General Partners (GPs), the professionals who manage and deploy that capital into high-growth startups.

LPs typically include large institutional investors such as pension funds, endowments, insurance companies, family offices, and high-net-worth individuals. They commit capital to a VC fund for several interconnected reasons. The primary motivation is financial: historically, top-quartile venture capital has delivered annualized net returns of 20 to 30%, far exceeding the long-term average of public equity markets. Beyond pure returns, LPs invest in VC for access to strategic co-investment opportunities, for diversification away from traditional asset classes like stocks and bonds, and to stay connected to emerging waves of innovation that could affect their broader business interests.

According to Cambridge Associates, the US Venture Capital Index delivered a net return of 6.2% in 2024, rebounding after two consecutive years of negative returns in 2022 and 2023. Over longer time horizons, the picture is more compelling: the index generated approximately 12 to 15% annualized returns over the 25-year period ending in 2023. From 1995 to 2020, the Cambridge Associates US VC Index generated an annualized average return of 32.4%, compared to 9.1% for the S&P 500 over the same period. The caveat, however, is that these averages mask enormous dispersion. The gap between top-quartile and bottom-quartile VC funds exceeds 30 percentage points in net IRR, far wider than in any other private equity strategy. In practice, getting into the right fund matters far more than simply allocating to the asset class.

"VC investments derive their large average returns from a very small chance of a huge payoff. Therefore, enjoying this average return without enormous risk requires a very diversified portfolio." — National Bureau of Economic Research

Why Do VCs Invest in Startups?

The logic behind venture capital investing is rooted in the asymmetric return profile of startups. While most startups fail, the ones that succeed can produce returns that are orders of magnitude greater than what is possible in public markets. The classic illustration of this dynamic is Facebook. When Accel Partners invested in Facebook in 2005 at a roughly $100 million valuation, the company was a scrappy college social network with no proven business model. When Facebook went public in 2012 at approximately $100 billion, Accel's investment had returned roughly 1,000 times its original value. By contrast, a public market investor who purchased Facebook shares at the IPO price would have earned approximately 3 times their money over the following decade: a respectable return, but nowhere near the venture-level upside.

This represents the fundamental reason venture capital exists as an asset class. VCs accept illiquidity, uncertainty, and high rates of failure in exchange for the possibility of backing the next transformative company at the earliest possible stage, when valuations are lowest and ownership stakes are largest.

~90% of startups ultimately fail. Yet a single breakout investment can return an entire fund many times over. This power-law dynamic is why portfolio construction and diversification are central to the VC model.
Sources: Harvard Business School / Failory Research, 2024

Why Invest in Multiple Startups?

Startups are cash-flow negative, they cannot survive without continuous outside capital. A single bad outcome, whether from technical failure, market rejection, or competitive disruption, can wipe out an investment entirely. In response to this reality, VCs build diversified portfolios, typically spanning 10 to 30 companies per fund that spread risk across many positions.

The statistical reality of venture returns makes this diversification essential. Data covering 21,640 venture financings from 2004 to 2013 shows that 64.8% of investments returned between zero and one times the invested capital, meaning they were effectively losses. Another 25.3% returned between 1 and 5 times capital. Just 5.9% returned 5 to 10 times, and only 0.4% returned more than 50 times. A hypothetical $100 million portfolio investing in 10 companies would produce approximately $206 million, or a 10% internal rate of return, at the median. Furthermore, if you remove the investments that returned more than 5 times capital from the calculation, that same portfolio falls to a negative return. This is the fundamental of venture investing, the entire fund is carried by a small number of outliers.

Harvard Business School research has found that roughly 75% of venture-backed startups never return cash to investors. In 30 to 40% of cases, investors lose their entire principal. This is how the system is built as the handful of breakout companies more than compensate for the many that never reached their potential.

How a VC Fund is Structured: The Five-Stage Process

The mechanics of running a venture capital fund follow a well-defined cycle that typically spans ten years, with optional one-year extensions. Understanding this cycle is essential to understanding how VCs make decisions, why they behave the way they do, and what motivates their interest in any given startup.

Stage 1
Fundraising

The process begins with the GP raising a fund from LPs. Capital is sourced from banks, corporations, endowments, sovereign wealth funds, and high-net-worth individuals. Fundraising is an ongoing activity; as one fund reaches the end of its investment period, the GP begins raising the next one. This creates a continuous pressure to demonstrate performance and maintain strong LP relationships.

Stage 2
Evaluation and Investment

Once a fund is raised, the GP begins evaluating startup opportunities and deploying capital. This phase, called the commitment period, typically covers the first five years of the fund's life. New investments are generally only made during this window. The process of evaluating a startup is covered in depth in the due diligence section below.

Stage 3
Monitoring

After an investment is made, the VC takes an active role in governing and steering the portfolio company toward profitable growth. This involves board participation, strategic advice, introductions to potential customers and partners, and help recruiting senior talent. Investing in a startup is just the beginning; supporting the founder as they build their business is how VCs drive higher returns.

Stage 4
Exit

Liquidity is generated through an exit event, which typically takes one of three forms: an initial public offering (IPO), a merger or acquisition (M&A), or, in the worst case, a failure and wind-down. According to data tracked by the Dow Jones VentureSource database, early-stage investors see roughly 12% of their portfolio companies exit via IPO and approximately 19% via M&A, with a failure rate of around 38% in companies reaching exit. Late-stage investors, who tend to back more mature companies, see higher M&A rates (50%) and IPO rates (60%), reflecting the more de-risked nature of their investments.

Stage 5
Distribution

After exits are realized, capital and investment proceeds are distributed back to both LPs and GPs according to a pre-agreed waterfall structure. This distribution marks the completion of the fund's lifecycle.

What is a Typical Life of a Venture Fund?

A standard venture fund has a ten-year life, though most LPA (Limited Partnership Agreements) allow for up to three one-year extensions, pushing the outer boundary to thirteen years. During the first five years, known as the commitment or investment period, the GP makes new investments. After year five, the fund shifts into its harvesting phase: no new companies are added to the portfolio, but the GP continues to make follow-on investments in existing portfolio companies and works actively toward exits.

Critically, a new fund is typically raised around year three to five of the existing fund, before the first fund's investment period concludes. This overlap means a GP is often simultaneously managing an existing portfolio while raising and deploying a new fund, requiring significant bandwidth and organizational infrastructure.

From the fund's perspective, three distinct phases define its life:

  • Formation (Year 0): Fundraising, legal structuring, and tax setup. The GP establishes the fund entity, negotiates LP terms, and handles all initial organizational work.
  • Investment (Years 0 to 5): Sourcing deals, conducting due diligence, deploying capital into new investments, and making early follow-on investments. This is the most active operational phase for the investment team.
  • Harvesting (Years 5 to 10+): Managing the portfolio toward exit, supporting portfolio company growth, executing follow-on investments into the strongest performers, and ultimately returning capital to LPs.

How Does a VC Make Money?

GPs are compensated through two mechanisms: a management fee and carried interest (commonly called "carry").

The management fee is typically 2% of committed capital per year, charged to cover the operational costs of running the fund, including salaries, rent, travel, and legal expenses. On a $100 million fund, this generates $2 million per year, or approximately $20 million over a ten-year fund life. This fee is not profit; it is an operational budget.

Carried interest is where GPs build real wealth. Carry is typically set at 20% of the fund's profits above a hurdle rate (also called a preferred return), which is usually around 8%. The mechanics work as follows, using a simplified example of a top-decile fund:

  • The fund raises $100 million from LPs.
  • After $20 million in fees and expenses, $80 million is invested into portfolio companies.
  • Of those investments, $20 million is written off as losses, leaving $60 million in active holdings.
  • The surviving portfolio grows to a value of $240 million, representing a 3.0x return on invested capital.
  • After returning the invested capital of $80 million and repaying the $20 million in fees, and satisfying any preferred return obligations, $120 million remains.
  • Of that $120 million, 80% ($96 million) goes to LPs and 20% ($24 million) goes to the GP as carried interest.

This structure aligns the interests of GPs and LPs: GPs only earn meaningful compensation when their investments generate real returns above the hurdle rate. A fund that merely returns capital earns the GP nothing beyond the management fee.

How to Source a Deal

Why Deal Sourcing Defines a VC Firm

In the world of venture capital, not all firms are created equal. The returns that separate a top-decile fund from a median are, in large part, a function of deal flow: the quantity, quality, and timing of investment opportunities that a firm is able to access. Deal sourcing is the front end of the entire venture capital machine, and without it, even the most sophisticated due diligence process has nothing to work with. That's why VC firms reach and cast as wide of a net as possible to truly understand if they are making the best possible investments in the market.

This dynamic is not lost on the industry's best practitioners. Top venture capital firms do not simply wait for founders to knock on their doors. They build proactive, multi-channel sourcing engines designed to identify the most promising startups as early as possible, often before those startups have any public profile at all.

How is the Competitive Landscape for Deal Sourcing?

According to data from the National Venture Capital Association and PitchBook, US VC managers completed over 14,600 deals in 2024 alone, deploying more than $213 billion in capital. Yet despite this volume, the fundamental reality of venture investing remains unchanged: the vast majority of returns come from a tiny fraction of companies.

This concentration of returns means that access to the right deals matters enormously. On top of that, the founders building those high-potential companies are aware of their leverage. The best founders often receive multiple term sheets simultaneously, and their decision about which firm to partner with is influenced by far more than valuation. Brand, network, operational support, and speed of execution all factor into a founder's choice of investor. This reality makes deal sourcing a two-sided competition: VCs compete not only to find the best startups, but to convince those startups to accept their capital over alternatives.

The ecosystem has become too large, too geographically distributed, and too fast-moving for any individual or small team to cover adequately through personal relationships alone. As a result, leading firms have invested heavily in building systematic, scalable sourcing infrastructure that complements traditional relationship-driven approaches with new tools, programs, and data capabilities.

Scout Programs

In a scout program, a VC firm partners with a distributed network of operators, founders, academics, and investors located in innovation hotspots around the world. These scouts are compensated, typically through a small allocation of carry in deals they source, to identify and refer high-potential founders to the firm before those founders have entered a formal fundraising process.

Sequoia Capital's scout program, one of the most well-known examples in the industry, has been credited with surfacing deals that would never have entered the firm's funnel through traditional channels. The program works because scouts have authentic, trusted relationships in their local communities that other VC firms simply cannot replicate from a distance. Furthermore, scouts bring diverse perspectives that can identify opportunities in underrepresented communities and non-traditional founder profiles, creating a more level playing field and democratizing access to capital for startups outside conventional networks.

Student Funds

Student funds represent a complementary strategy that targets the earliest possible stage of company formation: the university campus. Operated by trained university students, these vehicles deploy pre-seed capital to promising student founders who are building companies while still in school. Organizations like Dorm Room Fund and Front Row Ventures are prominent examples of this model in North America. Student fund programs also help nurture the next generation of venture talent, creating a pipeline of trained analysts and associates who understand the firm's investment philosophy.

Venture Studios

Venture studios, sometimes called startup studios or company builders, create, build, and launch multiple startups simultaneously from within the organization itself. The studio provides a full suite of shared resources: product teams, designers, legal infrastructure, marketing capabilities, and access to an expert network, all deployed in support of multiple nascent companies at once.

For VC firms that operate or partner with venture studios, the model offers several compelling advantages. The startup process is de-risked because experienced operators are involved from the earliest stage, applying learnings from previous company builds. Shared resources create economies of scale that reduce burn rates across the portfolio. The survival rate of studio-built companies tends to be higher than that of independently founded startups, reflecting the structural support the model provides.

Feeder Funds

Feeder funds allow established VC firms to extend their reach by investing in emerging fund managers who are deploying capital in markets, geographies, or verticals by backing smaller, specialized funds and gaining access to their deal flow, learnings, and portfolio companies as a result.

Inovia Capital's Discovery Fund, which focuses on investing in emerging managers to strengthen the Canadian tech funding ecosystem, is a prominent example of this model in practice. The fund creates a win-win structure: emerging managers gain access to capital and institutional credibility, while Inovia gains diversified exposure to a broader set of companies and markets than it could access through direct investment alone.

Service Offerings

Some of the world's largest VC firms have built entire platforms of services designed to attract founders and create ongoing touchpoints with the startup ecosystem. Andreessen Horowitz (a16z) offers portfolio companies operational support, advisory services through a library of company-building content, platform services and technical expertise, and access to curated networks and events. These services function as a form of deal sourcing, because they create a reason for founders to engage with the firm even before they are raising capital.

EIRs and Referral Networks

Entrepreneurs in Residence (EIRs) are seasoned operators or former founders who join a firm for a defined period, during which they may provide operational feedback to existing portfolio companies, evaluate new investment opportunities, or work toward founding a new company that the firm will invest in. The EIR model's biggest advantage from a deal sourcing perspective is that when an EIR does create a new startup, the founding team arrives pre-vetted.

According to research by Zapflow, referral networks consistently produce the highest-quality investment opportunities available to VC firms, precisely because the referring party has already applied an informal layer of screening and is putting their own reputation on the line by making the introduction.

Conferences and Networking Events

Conferences aggregate investors, founders, service providers, and ecosystem players in environments designed for informal connection and discovery. Regular presence at key events builds the firm's reputation and brand within the ecosystem, signaling seriousness and commitment to a sector or geography. The compounding effect of consistent event presence over time is one of the more underappreciated elements of a strong sourcing strategy. Especially in the age of AI and automation, "in real life" is the true differentiator as human connection cannot be accelerated or automated.

Steps in the Investment Process

Investing in startups is a disciplined process that moves from identifying an opportunity to becoming a trusted partner in a founder's journey. Each step helps build conviction and competitive advantage.

Step 1
Sourcing the Deal

Top VCs build multi-channel sourcing engines: referral networks, scout programs, student funds, conferences, and data-driven inbound platforms. The goal is to see the best opportunities early, before competitive pressure drives up price and reduces ownership.

Step 2
Thesis Fit

Before spending meaningful time, a VC must ask a single question: does this company fit within our fund's investment mandate? Thesis alignment covers stage, sector, geography, and check size. A deal that is objectively strong but outside the fund's swim lanes should be passed on, as misaligned investments dilute expertise and strain portfolio resources.

Step 3
Due Diligence

Due diligence is targeted, not exhaustive. The core areas are: the team (the most important factor, cited by 95% of VCs as a primary criterion), the market (large, growing, and underserved), the competition (defensible differentiation), the product (evidence of genuine value creation), the business model (sound unit economics and a scalable go-to-market), and the risks. The process is probabilistic, not deterministic: the goal is to form a well-supported investment thesis, not to eliminate all uncertainty.

Step 4
The Investment Committee

Deals are approved either through a conviction model, where a single partner can champion and close an investment, or a consensus model, where a majority or all partners must agree. In either case, the sponsoring partner must articulate a clear investment thesis and a defensible return case. The best IC presentations pre-empt objections and demonstrate that the hardest questions have already been asked.

Step 5
Winning the Founder

The best founders choose their investors as carefully as investors choose them. A VC wins allocation by demonstrating genuine value beyond capital: relevant network introductions, sector expertise, operational guidance, and a track record of supporting founders through difficult moments. Founders want a partner, not just a check.

Step 6
Winning Allocation

In competitive rounds, allocation is earned through speed, relationship quality, and perceived value-add. Being first to term sheet, offering favorable terms relative to other bidders, or providing a unique strategic asset such as a key customer introduction or a board member with direct category expertise can determine whether a fund gets its target ownership or is crowded out.

Step 7
Portfolio Value Creation

Post-investment value comes through active board participation, strategic hiring support, customer and investor introductions, and honest operational guidance. The VC's return is ultimately a function of the founder's success, making post-investment support both a fiduciary and a reputational obligation.

Together, these seven steps describe not a linear checklist but a compounding relationship between rigor, judgment, and trust.

How Startups Can Extract Value from VC Firms

There is a well-documented gap at the heart of venture capital. According to the Forward More Than Money report (2021), 92% of VC firms describe themselves as value-add investors. Only 61% of founders agree, and 65% report that their investors tried but missed the mark. The implication is clear: providing value beyond capital is not automatic. It must be structured, intentional, and genuinely founder-centric.

1. Network and Customer Introductions

The most consistently valued form of VC support is access to networks. Research shows that 33% of founders point to an investor's connections as a primary contributor to business success (Forward More Than Money, 2021). This includes introductions to prospective customers, strategic partners, and distribution channels. A VC who opens one meaningful enterprise relationship can accelerate a startup's go-to-market by months. Crucially, 63% of US-based founders cite customer introductions as among the top three factors they consider when selecting a VC.

2. Fundraising Support

The second most valued contribution is help raising future capital. Founders view VCs as natural bridges to the next round. A VC's warm introductions to co-investors, its signal of continued confidence through pro-rata participation, and its coaching on investor narrative and data room preparation can be decisive in a competitive fundraising environment.

3. Talent Acquisition

Hiring is the single largest operational challenge for most early-stage companies. According to research highlighted in Forbes (2025), portfolio companies that receive active talent support from their VCs reduce time-to-hire by 40% and improve executive acceptance rates by 60%. VCs can leverage their networks to source candidates, reduce reliance on expensive recruiters, and help founders articulate equity stories that attract top-tier talent.

4. Strategic and Operational Guidance

VC investors bring cross-portfolio pattern recognition that founders, operating within a single company, cannot replicate. This includes playbooks on pricing, sales motion, product development prioritization, and crisis management. ScienceDirect research confirms that VC-backed firms with active investor monitoring demonstrate significantly higher productivity growth in the years following first-round investment, a benefit attributed specifically to coaching and value-adding services rather than capital alone.

5. Board and Governance Support

Effective board participation translates investor experience into structured accountability. VCs who come prepared, challenge assumptions constructively, and help set credible operating milestones give founders a governance infrastructure that scales with the company. The ScienceDirect literature on VC monitoring further establishes that closer VC-CEO interaction is most valuable at the earliest stages, when uncertainty is highest and the need for external perspective is greatest.

6. Peer Learning and Portfolio Community

A well-run VC firm creates value at the portfolio level, not just the company level. Connecting founders across similar growth challenges, facilitating knowledge transfer between portfolio companies, and organizing events that foster peer learning are increasingly recognized as meaningful differentiators. The firms that close the gap between what they promise and what founders experience are the ones that systematize post-investment support rather than leaving it to chance.
